## Why this book

| Reach | Note |
|---|---|
| Goodreads | 4.15 / 5 across ~163,000 ratings |
| Published | 2016 — has had time to prove durability |
| Genre | Productivity / knowledge-work focus |
| Author follow-up | A World Without Email (2021), Slow Productivity (2024) — Newport keeps refining the same lens |

Cal Newport's central claim: the ability to focus without distraction on cognitively demanding tasks is becoming **simultaneously rare and valuable**. The book is a manual for cultivating that ability.

---

## Core lens (≤2 keywords) — `shallow audit` + `4 philosophies`

These are the two pieces that don't appear in any other distill skill and uniquely belong to Newport's frame:

1. **Shallow audit** — quantify what percentage of your workday is shallow vs deep. Most knowledge workers run 70-90% shallow and never realize it. The audit alone shifts behavior.
2. **4 philosophies of scheduling** — Monastic / Bimodal / Rhythmic / Journalistic. The book's most actionable contribution: pick one that matches your life shape, don't pick by what sounds heroic.

Concepts in the book that **don't** become lenses:
- "Embrace boredom" → too abstract for cold-start
- "Quit social media" → categorical rule, not a lens
- "Productive meditation" → advanced, not entry
- "Memorize a deck of cards" → demo of method, not the method

## 15-second cold-start sketch (3 candidates, will pick strongest)

**Candidate A · digital tidying (most universal)**  
> Pick the next 30 minutes. Right now, close every tab and app except one. Phone face down, arm's length away.  
> Newport calls this 'draining the shallows' — not for the next 30 minutes, but as the default state of work.

**Candidate B · the audit (most distinctive to Deep Work)**  
> Look at yesterday. Roughly, what % of your work hours was deep vs shallow?  
> If you can't answer in 5 seconds, the answer is "I don't know" — and the book says that's the most common starting state.

**Candidate C · the 90-minute timer (most Newport-pure)**  
> Set a timer for 90 minutes. Don't start it. Just set it.  
> Newport's claim: someone who can hold attention through a 90-minute deep block is in the top 5% of knowledge workers in 2026. The timer is the entry test.

Recommended: **A** as primary (works for everyone, immediate body action) + **B** baked into follow-up turn (the audit drives state-first detection).

## 3 trigger user utterances

- "I can't focus / I keep getting distracted / I scroll too much when I should be working"
- "I worked all day but didn't get the important thing done"
- "I have a big project but I keep doing small things instead"

Optional secondary:
- "I'm in meetings all day and nothing real happens"
- "I want to do focused work but my job won't let me"

---

## not_for list (mandatory; ≥2 items + ≥1 crisis route)

- **ADHD / executive dysfunction** — the book treats focus as a discipline problem. For diagnosed ADHD it underestimates the medical/neurological dimension. Route to a clinician.
- **Burnout** — "deep work" requires energy. A burned-out person is in pre-recovery, not pre-focus. Route to rest, not to scheduling.
- **Knowledge-work skeptical** — if the user's job is overwhelmingly shallow by structural design (e.g. fast-food, retail floor, ER triage), the book doesn't apply. Acknowledge and exit, don't force-fit.
- **Workplace harassment / toxic environment** — if the user can't focus because of unsafe conditions, route to HR / labor counsel, not to scheduling.

## Why this book vs adjacent books in archetype

This is the **first cognitive/focus archetype book** in the repo. Closest competitors:

| Adjacent book | Why Deep Work wins as first | When the other should be added |
|---|---|---|
| **Stolen Focus** (Hari, 2022) | Hari diagnoses the problem (collective + environmental). Newport gives the operating manual (individual + procedural). Better entry point. | v0.5 — when we have a "diagnosis vs prescription" pair |
| **Make It Stick** (Brown et al., 2014) | Different problem space — retention, not focus. Worth its own slot. | v0.8 — learning-specific scenarios |
| **Hyperfocus** (Bailey, 2018) | Lighter, less rigorous, less load-bearing methodology. Newport has the framework muscle. | Skip — Deep Work covers it |
| **A World Without Email** (Newport, 2021) | Same author, narrower scope (email/comms). Deep Work is the foundational lens. | v1.5 — companion skill if Deep Work is successful |

**Risk of overlap with Atomic Habits**: Both books touch behavior change. Distinguishing them in the meta-skill is critical:
- AH's lens = "atomic unit + identity vote" (behavior systems)
- DW's lens = "shallow audit + 4 philosophies" (cognitive scarcity + scheduling)
- A user saying "I want to focus better" should route to DW, not AH. Trigger words handle this.

## Archetype: cognitive / focus

First book in this archetype. Validates whether the meta-skill framework adapts to:
- Books whose lens is **diagnostic-then-prescriptive** (audit reveals the state, philosophy chooses the path)
- Books whose cold-start is **environmental** (close tabs, set timer) vs **identity** (you're a reader)
- Books where the "minimum action" is harder to define (focus isn't reducible to "1 page" the way reading is)

If Deep Work works cleanly, framework is validated cross-archetype.  
If it has to bend the meta-skill, we learn where the framework's edges are.

## Boot-message sketch (will refine in Phase 3)

```
I'm the Deep Work skill. I help you make space for focused work in a
day that wants to stay shallow.

The whole flow is about 3-5 minutes, in 3 stages:
  1. 30-second warm-up (close one set of distractions)
  2. Audit: rough % of your work that's deep vs shallow
  3. One scheduling philosophy + one concrete next session

Want to do any of the following first?
  A. Paste 1-2 lines about your work (job type / hours / current
     biggest distraction)
  B. Pick a scenario:
     1) Can't focus, scrolling instead
     2) Meeting-heavy day, nothing real gets done
     3) Big project stalled, small things winning
     4) Job is structurally shallow, what now
  C. Just say what's happening

I'll wait.
```
