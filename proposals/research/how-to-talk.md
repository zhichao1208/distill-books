# Research package: How to Talk So Kids Will Listen & Listen So Kids Will Talk

*Adele Faber & Elaine Mazlish · Avon / Scribner / Harper · 1980 original; 30th-anniversary updated edition 2012 · ~286 pages*

## Facts

- **Authors.** Adele Faber (BA NYU, MA Education Hunter College) and Elaine Mazlish (BA NYU, MA Education Bank Street College) — both worked under **Dr. Haim Ginott** in his parent-guidance groups in New York in the 1960s-70s. Ginott (Israeli-American child psychologist, author of *Between Parent and Child*, 1965) is the direct intellectual parent of the methodology; Faber and Mazlish credit him on the dedication page and throughout. They began running their own workshops in 1970, published their first book *Liberated Parents, Liberated Children* in 1974, then *How to Talk So Kids Will Listen* in 1980. Mazlish died in 2017; Faber died in 2024.
- **Sales and adoption.** Over 3 million copies sold; translated into 30+ languages. New York Times bestseller. Used as a parent-class staple in pediatric offices, school PTAs, religious congregations, and Head Start programs for 40+ years. The Faber/Mazlish workshop format (now run by Joanna Faber, Adele's daughter, and Julie King through the *How To Talk* group programs) is taught by certified facilitators in 30+ countries.
- **Format.** Six chapters, each built around one tool-cluster, with **cartoon strips** (drawn by Kimberly Ann Coe) showing wrong-way and right-way parent-child exchanges side by side, plus practice exercises, parent stories from workshops, and a "remember" recap. The cartoon-strip format is the book's signature visual device and one reason it stuck — the methods are seen, not just read.
- **Follow-ups.** *Siblings Without Rivalry* (1987) extends the methods to sibling conflict. *How To Talk So Teens Will Listen* (2005) adapts them for adolescence. *How To Talk So Little Kids Will Listen* (2017, by Joanna Faber and Julie King) is the under-7 application.
- **Goodreads.** Average ~4.27 across ~62,000 ratings; consistently in the top 5 parenting books since the platform began tracking. Reviews are mostly enthusiastic, with a recurring critical note that the technique can feel "scripted" or "manipulative" if applied verbatim — a critique the book itself anticipates.

## Core methodology

The book is structured as **five tool-clusters plus one autonomy chapter and one praise chapter**. Each tool is a specific verbal move with a named alternative-to-default-parenting purpose. The thesis: most parent-child conflicts are not about the surface issue (homework, screens, the coat) — they are about the child's feelings being denied or the parent's request being delivered through ordering, blaming, threatening, or labeling. Change the moves; the conflicts shrink.

### Chapter 1 — Helping children deal with their feelings

The foundation. Before any cooperation, limit-setting, or problem-solving, **acknowledge the feeling**. Four moves:

1. **Listen with full attention.** Stop the dishes, turn away from the phone.
2. **Acknowledge their feelings with a word.** "Oh." "Mmm." "I see." Container, not interrogation.
3. **Give the feeling a name.** "That sounds frustrating." "You're really disappointed."
4. **Give the child their wishes in fantasy.** "I wish I could make the candy store open right now."

The anti-pattern they target: denial ("you're fine," "stop crying, it's not a big deal"), philosophical response ("life isn't fair, get used to it"), advice ("just ignore him"), pity ("oh you poor thing"), amateur psychoanalysis ("you're tired, that's why you're upset"). All of these communicate *your feeling is wrong*, which escalates the feeling rather than resolves it.

### Chapter 2 — Engaging cooperation

Five alternatives to the default mode of ordering, blaming, warning, lecturing, moralizing, name-calling, threatening, prophesying ("you'll never amount to anything"), comparing ("why can't you be like your sister"), and sarcasm:

1. **Describe what you see.** "There's a wet towel on the bed." (Not "you always leave wet towels everywhere.")
2. **Give information.** "Wet towels get the sheets damp." (Not "how many times have I told you.")
3. **Say it with a word.** "Towel." (Replaces a paragraph of lecture.)
4. **Talk about your feelings.** "I get upset when I sit on a wet bed." (Not "you're so inconsiderate.")
5. **Write a note.** Especially effective with school-age kids and pre-teens, who experience parent voice as nagging but parent handwriting as quaint.

The principle: speak about the **situation** or **yourself**, not the child's character. Character labels stick and shape identity over time.

### Chapter 3 — Alternatives to punishment

The most counter-cultural chapter for many readers. The argument: punishment **distracts** the child from feeling sorry about what they did (now they're feeling sorry for themselves, or angry at the parent), interrupts the natural consequence of misbehavior (regret + repair), and damages the relationship that is the actual lever for long-term cooperation. The seven alternatives:

1. **Point out a way to be helpful.** "It would be a big help if you'd hold the bag while I unpack."
2. **Express strong disapproval — without attacking character.** "I am furious that the milk got left out and now we have to throw it away."
3. **State your expectations.** "I expect that when you borrow something, you return it."
4. **Show the child how to make amends.** "The carpet needs damp-sponging now."
5. **Give a choice.** "You can walk like a person or I'll put you in the cart — you choose."
6. **Take action.** Pick up the child. Put the toys away for the day. Action, not lecture.
7. **Problem-solve.** Sit together. Talk about the child's feelings, the parent's feelings, brainstorm solutions, write them down, pick one to try.

### Chapter 4 — Encouraging autonomy

Six moves that build the child's separateness — the long-term goal of parenting being a child who can run their own life:

1. **Let them make choices.** Even small ones — gray socks or white socks — at age three.
2. **Show respect for their struggle.** "Jars can be hard to open. Sometimes it helps to tap the lid with a spoon."
3. **Don't ask too many questions.** ("How was school? Did you eat? Who did you sit with?" — interrogation.)
4. **Don't rush to answer questions.** "That's an interesting question. What do you think?"
5. **Encourage them to use sources outside the home.** Pet store, librarian, dentist's hygienist.
6. **Don't take away hope.** Don't pre-disappoint them about a tryout, a contest, a friendship.

### Chapter 5 — Praise

The Dweck-adjacent chapter, published 26 years before Carol Dweck's *Mindset*. The argument: evaluative praise ("good girl," "you're so smart," "what a great drawing") can backfire — it teaches the child to perform for the evaluator, makes them anxious about losing the label, and feels patronizing when it doesn't match the child's own assessment. Three moves replace it:

1. **Describe what you see.** "I see a clean desk, sharpened pencils, and books lined up on the shelf."
2. **Describe what you feel.** "It's a pleasure to walk into this room."
3. **Sum up praiseworthy behavior with a word.** "Now that's what I call organization."

The child does the evaluating themselves — *"I'm organized"* — and that internal label is durable in a way the external one never is.

### Chapter 6 — Freeing children from playing roles

Antidote to the family-script trap (the "shy one," the "bossy one," the "clumsy one"). Six moves: look for opportunities to show the child a new picture of themselves; put them in situations where they can see themselves differently; let them overhear you saying something positive about them; model the behavior you want; be a storehouse for their special moments; when they act according to the old label, state your feelings and expectations.

## Critique / honesty — IMPORTANT

The tools work. The empirical foundation is 30 years of workshop case studies under Ginott and Faber/Mazlish, not RCTs. Be honest about both.

- **Pre-modern evidence base.** The book pre-dates the 1990s-2000s wave of empirical child-development research (attachment theory operationalization, affective neuroscience, Dweck's mindset work, the Gottman child-rearing studies). The methods *align* with much of what came later — affect-labeling research (Lieberman 2007), process-praise research (Mueller & Dweck 1998; Gunderson et al. 2013), authoritative-parenting outcomes (Baumrind, then Steinberg) — but the book was not designed against those literatures. It was distilled from clinical observation. Use it as **clinically-rich heuristic**, not as evidence-based protocol.
- **The "scripted feel" problem.** The most common complaint in critical reviews and from couples therapists is that beginners apply the moves verbatim and children (especially pre-teens and teens) react with "stop talking to me like a therapist." The book itself warns about this — Faber and Mazlish say the relationship matters more than the script, that the words are scaffolding to be internalized and dropped. But the workshop format encourages script practice, and many readers stop at the script stage. Same failure mode as NVC.
- **Manipulative-when-misused risk.** "Describe what you see" can shade into surveillance ("I see a kitchen that hasn't been cleaned"). "Talk about your feelings" can shade into emotional pressure ("I feel hurt when you don't text back"). The methods presuppose **good faith** — a parent using them in pursuit of the child's autonomy. A parent using them in pursuit of compliance experiences them as failing, and the child experiences them as worse than honest ordering.
- **Developmental-difference scope.** The book is written for typically-developing children in low-acuity moments. For children with **autism spectrum, ADHD, sensory processing disorder, anxiety disorders, OCD, attachment disruption from early trauma, or PDA (pathological demand avoidance)**, the methods often need significant adaptation or do not work at all. "Give a choice" can overwhelm an autistic child in sensory overload. "Acknowledge feelings" can re-trigger a trauma response. "Problem-solve together" presupposes executive function the child may not yet have. For these situations the book is not enough — see Ross Greene's *The Explosive Child*, Mona Delahooke's *Beyond Behaviors*, or seek clinical support.
- **Co-parenting / abuse-context limit.** Same warning as NVC. In families with an abusive or coercive co-parent, the more-empathic parent doing all the feeling-acknowledgment and problem-solving while the other parent ignores or punishes creates emotional whiplash for the child and exhausts the empathic parent. The methods presuppose a relationship; they do not create one across coercion.
- **Cultural-context concern.** Developed in 1970s middle-class urban American parenting culture. The autonomy chapter especially assumes a developmental telos of individual independence — let them choose, let them struggle, let them seek outside sources. In **East Asian, South Asian, Middle Eastern, Latin American, and other family cultures** where filial interdependence and hierarchical respect are the developmental goal, several moves translate poorly without adaptation. "Don't ask too many questions" reads as parental disinterest in a culture where attentive inquiry signals love. "Encourage outside sources" can read as undermining family authority. The **feelings-acknowledgment and praise** chapters translate better; the **autonomy** chapter needs the most local rewriting.
- **What the book does NOT cover.** Sleep training. Feeding/eating problems. Screen-time policy. Medical conditions. Divorce/custody dynamics. Religious upbringing. Co-parenting alignment. The book is narrowly about parent-child verbal exchange in the moment.
- **Validation by adoption, not by trial.** 40 years of staying in print, being taught in workshops, and being recommended pediatrician-to-parent is a strong signal but not the same as RCT evidence. Treat the book as a senior practitioner handing you their playbook, not as a clinical protocol.

**Bottom line for the skill.** The five tool-clusters (feelings, cooperation, alternatives to punishment, autonomy, praise) are genuinely useful for ordinary parent-child moments in typically-developing kids. The skill should teach them crisply, refuse to apply them to clinical situations (autism / ADHD / trauma / abuse) without routing to a clinician, warn against the script-stage robot voice, and acknowledge cultural-translation needs.

## Common reader use cases

- Toddler / preschooler refusing to put on a coat, get in the car, leave the playground
- School-age child who "won't listen" — repeated requests, escalating volume
- Pre-teen withdrawing — one-word answers, closed bedroom door
- Sibling fighting (the bigger book is *Siblings Without Rivalry*, but this one covers the basics)
- Parent who keeps yelling and wants to stop — the book is also a script for the parent's own restraint
- Homework battles
- Picky eating / food refusal moments (not the medical problem)
- "Why won't they just do what I say"
- "I've tried everything and nothing works"
- Parent feeling stuck in the lecture/threat/punish/repeat loop
- Teacher / grandparent / nanny who wants to communicate with kids differently
- Parent of a child labeled "shy" / "the bossy one" / "the lazy one" trying to break the family script

## Adjacent books

- ***Between Parent and Child*** (Haim Ginott, 1965) — the source. Faber/Mazlish are explicitly Ginott's students. Worth reading for the philosophical foundation.
- ***Siblings Without Rivalry*** (Faber/Mazlish, 1987) — direct sequel; sibling-conflict application. Often paired.
- ***How To Talk So Little Kids Will Listen*** (Joanna Faber & Julie King, 2017) — under-7 application by Adele's daughter and her teaching partner. More tactical for the toddler/preschool years.
- ***The Whole-Brain Child*** (Siegel & Bryson, 2011) — neuroscience companion. The two books pair extraordinarily well: Faber/Mazlish give the **exact words**, Siegel/Bryson explain the **why** in brain terms. The parenting-archetype pair for this slate.
- ***No-Drama Discipline*** (Siegel & Bryson, 2014) — Siegel/Bryson's direct take on the alternatives-to-punishment terrain.
- ***The Explosive Child*** (Ross Greene, 1998) — collaborative problem-solving for higher-acuity / clinical situations. Where Faber/Mazlish stops, Greene begins.
- ***Beyond Behaviors*** (Mona Delahooke, 2019) — polyvagal-informed alternative for neurodivergent children and trauma backgrounds.
- ***Hunt, Gather, Parent*** (Michaeleen Doucleff, 2021) — cross-cultural critique of WEIRD parenting; useful counterweight to the autonomy chapter's individualist assumptions.
- ***Hold On to Your Kids*** (Neufeld & Maté, 2004) — attachment lens on peer-orientation, complements the Faber/Mazlish relationship-first stance.
- ***Mindset*** (Carol Dweck, 2006) — the research-backed full treatment of the process-praise principle that Faber/Mazlish stated in 1980.
- ***Nonviolent Communication*** (Marshall Rosenberg, 1999) — adult-relationship cousin. Same Carl-Rogers / Ginott humanistic-psychology family tree.
