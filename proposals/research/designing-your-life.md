# Research package: Designing Your Life

**Book**: *Designing Your Life: How to Build a Well-Lived, Joyful Life*
**Authors**: Bill Burnett & Dave Evans
**Publisher**: Alfred A. Knopf, September 2016
**ISBN**: 9781101875322
**Length**: ~272 pages

## Facts

- **#1 New York Times bestseller** (Advice / How-To / Miscellaneous), 2016. Sustained presence on the list for multiple weeks after release; reissued repeatedly with workbook (2020) and *Build the Perfect Career* (2018) and *Designing Your Work Life* (2020) follow-ons.
- **Goodreads**: ~3.90 / 5 across the main edition with ~23,000+ ratings and ~2,000 written reviews. Distribution skews three-and-four-star with a notable long tail of one- and two-star reviews citing privilege, Stanford-centric framing, and thin examples. The rating is meaningfully lower than peers in the same shelf (*So Good* 4.05, *Range* 4.13, *Atomic Habits* 4.35).
- **Origin**: grew out of the Stanford d.school course ENGR104B "Designing Your Life," first offered in 2007. The course became one of the most popular electives on campus and seeded the Stanford Life Design Lab (founded 2015), which Burnett directs.
- **Translation reach**: 30+ languages; Knopf reports >1M copies across editions and follow-ups, though no per-title number is published.
- **Author backgrounds**:
  - **Bill Burnett**: BS/MS Product Design at Stanford; seven years at Apple including the PowerBook program (Director of the PowerBook business unit); product-design work at Hasbro on original Star Wars action figures; Executive Director of the Stanford Design Program.
  - **Dave Evans**: MS Mechanical Engineering at Stanford; led product marketing for Apple's first mouse and laser printing rollout; first VP of Talent at Electronic Arts (left Apple with EA founder Trip Hawkins); later Consulting Assistant Professor at Stanford; co-founder of designingyour.life and the Life Design Studio for educators.
- **Commercial ecosystem after the book**: designingyour.life (workshops, coaching certifications, "Designing Your New Life After Loss" programs), Stanford Continuing Studies certificate, Life Design Studio for Educators (300+ institutions trained), corporate workshops at 2.5-day intensives (~$1,500–$3,500 USD per seat).

## Core methodology

The book is structured as 11 chapters built around five mindsets (curiosity, bias-to-action, reframing, awareness, radical collaboration) and roughly a dozen concrete exercises. The argument: most people approach life with engineering thinking (find the right answer) when they should use design thinking (prototype many candidate answers, test them, iterate). The intellectual debt is to Stanford d.school and to Rittel & Webber's 1973 "wicked problem" framing.

### The five mindsets (Ch. 1)
- **Curiosity** — treat the world as a question, not a verdict.
- **Bias to action** — ideas are cheap, prototypes are how you learn.
- **Reframing** — when stuck, change the question, not the answer.
- **Awareness** — life design is a process, not a one-shot decision.
- **Radical collaboration** — your life is co-designed with the people in it.

### Wicked vs tame life problems (Ch. 1)
Burnett & Evans import Horst Rittel and Melvin Webber's 1973 *Policy Sciences* paper ("Dilemmas in a General Theory of Planning") and apply it to life decisions. Wicked problems have no definitive formulation, no stopping rule, no true-or-false solution, no immediate test of success. The book argues most life questions — what career, what city, what relationships — are wicked, and engineering-style optimization fails on them.

### Compass — Workview and Lifeview (Ch. 2)
Two short essays the reader writes: a Workview ("why work?") and a Lifeview ("why life?"), then a coherence check between them. The Compass is the constant reference point during later exercises.

### Wayfinding — Good Time Journal (Ch. 3)
The book's energy/engagement tracking tool. For 2–4 weeks the reader logs daily activities and rates each on **engagement** (was I drawn in?) and **energy** (did this give or drain energy?). Reflection prompts surface patterns: which activities reliably engage, which drain, what triggers "flow." Adapted from positive-psychology engagement research (Csikszentmihalyi flow; Seligman's PERMA) but presented as a design-research tool, not a psychometric instrument.

### Getting Unstuck — mind maps and anchor problems (Ch. 4)
- **Mind maps**: pick a high-engagement activity from the journal, free-associate three rings of related concepts, mash up unexpected branches into role concepts.
- **Anchor problems**: real, hard problems the reader has been stuck on for months/years. The fix is to reframe — almost always you're solving the wrong problem.
- **Gravity problems**: situations that are unchangeable (you can't undo your age, your degree, the economy). The book's prescription is acceptance, not solution.

### Odyssey Plans (Ch. 5) — the signature exercise
Three five-year futures, each sketched on a single page:
1. **Life One** — the path you're currently on, taken seriously.
2. **Life Two** — what you'd do if Life One were suddenly impossible (industry collapses, role disappears).
3. **Life Three** — what you'd do if money and image were no object.
Each plan includes a timeline of milestones, a title, three questions the plan raises, and four-axis self-ratings (resources, likability, confidence, coherence with Compass). The point is not to pick one; it's to surface patterns across the three.

### Prototyping (Ch. 6) — Life Design Interviews
Two prototyping modes:
- **Prototype conversations** (a.k.a. Life Design Interviews): structured informational interviews with people already doing the work you're considering, framed as design research rather than networking. Goal is the person's story, not a job lead.
- **Prototype experiences**: small, time-boxed, low-cost trials — shadow a day, take a class, freelance a weekend, attend a conference.
The methodological move is treating uncertainty about a path as a hypothesis to test cheaply rather than a decision to deliberate.

### How not to get a job (Ch. 7)
Critique of the visible job market. Burnett & Evans cite a claimed 80% "hidden job market" figure (sourced loosely to industry surveys, not formally validated) and prescribe networking-through-conversation as the primary search method, not online applications.

### Designing your dream job (Ch. 8) and choosing happiness (Ch. 9)
- Reframes "dream job" from a fixed destination to an iteratively designed match.
- Distinguishes **gathering** (generate many options) from **choosing** (commit to one, agonize less). Cites Sheena Iyengar's choice-overload research and Dan Gilbert on the affective forecasting error — humans systematically overestimate how much they'll suffer from foreclosing options.

### Failure immunity (Ch. 10)
A reframe inherited from design culture: failures are data, not verdicts. Three failure types — screw-ups (do the same thing better), weaknesses (recurring failure modes you accept and route around), and growth opportunities (the ones worth designing for).

### Building a team (Ch. 11)
Radical collaboration: a small group (3–5 people) of "allies and players" who actually do the work with you, plus mentors. The book argues life design is not a solo activity and the lone-genius framing fails.

### Borrowed frames the book doesn't fully credit
- Design-thinking pipeline (empathize → define → ideate → prototype → test) — IDEO / Stanford d.school canon, not invented here.
- Wicked problems — Rittel & Webber 1973.
- Flow, engagement — Csikszentmihalyi.
- Choice overload — Iyengar.
- Affective forecasting — Daniel Gilbert.

## Critique / honesty

- **Affluent-bias and network-bias are the dominant critique.** The Odyssey Plans, prototype conversations, and prototype experiences all presuppose: a working professional network you can ask for informational interviews; geographic mobility to "go shadow someone"; financial slack to take a class, a weekend, or a sabbatical to "prototype"; career flexibility that does not exist for caregivers, gig workers, hourly workers, recent immigrants, or people in declining industries. Multiple Goodreads and blog reviewers note the book "is most helpful if you're already a professional in a metropolitan area" and that Stanford references read as snobbery. The book itself acknowledges its Stanford-classroom origin but does not seriously engage with what changes when readers don't have the Stanford starting kit.
- **Empirical evidence base is thin.** Unlike *Drive*, *Make It Stick*, or *Peak* — where most claims trace to controlled experiments — Designing Your Life's tools (mind maps, anchor problems, Odyssey Plans, Wayfinding journal) are practitioner heuristics inherited from design-thinking pedagogy, not validated psychological instruments. There is no published RCT, no longitudinal cohort study, no peer-reviewed outcomes paper from Stanford Life Design Lab on the book's methodology as deployed at scale. Course evaluations and self-reported satisfaction surveys are not the same as evidence.
- **The "design thinking as universal methodology" claim is contested in the design-research literature itself.** Lucy Kimbell ("Rethinking Design Thinking," *Design and Culture* 2011/2012) argues the field has drifted into anecdote-heavy claims unsupported by theory or empirics, and centers the designer above the user. Natasha Iskander (*Harvard Business Review*, September 2018, "Design Thinking Is Fundamentally Conservative and Preserves the Status Quo") argues design thinking privileges incremental, risk-averse choices framed as innovation, and substitutes a thin reading of user needs for substantive structural analysis. Both critiques apply directly to Life Design: framing a structurally constrained life (caregiving, debt, immigration status, illness) as a design problem to prototype around can mislabel the constraint as a creativity failure.
- **Corporate-pitch concern.** The book is also a funnel. designingyour.life sells coaching certifications, multi-day workshops at $1,500–$3,500 per seat, and corporate engagements; Stanford Continuing Studies sells a certificate; the Life Design Studio for Educators trains paid cohorts. None of this is hidden, but it shapes which findings get amplified — positive testimonials are content marketing, negative outcomes are not surveyed. Compare *Range* or *Peak*, where the author has no comparable downstream product to sell.
- **Generalization beyond Stanford is asserted, not demonstrated.** The Life Design Lab cites 300+ partner institutions, but there is no published comparison of outcomes across institution types (R1 vs community college vs adult-education). The book's case studies are predominantly white-collar, mid-to-upper-class, U.S.-coastal. The course was built for and tested on students who could, by default, take an elective on what to do with their life — a population already at the high end of choice and capital.
- **"Follow your energy" inherits a passion-trap variant.** Burnett & Evans deliberately avoid the "follow your passion" trap that Cal Newport attacks in *So Good They Can't Ignore You* — they explicitly say passion is an output of competence. But the Wayfinding journal centers personal *engagement and energy* as the primary signal, which is the same underlying heuristic at one remove. A reader who finds nothing in their current life energizing (depression, burnout, a job they took for survival) is told to keep journaling. The book does not seriously address the case where the energy signal is broken.
- **Anchor-problem vs gravity-problem cut is shaky in practice.** The book's distinction — anchor problems are reframeable, gravity problems must be accepted — has no operational test. In practice the distinction collapses into "the kind of problem I can solve with this book is the kind of problem this book addresses." Caregiving for an aging parent, chronic illness, sole earner constraints — the book either reclassifies these as gravity problems (accept) or as design problems (prototype around), and the choice of label drives the prescription.
- **Bias to action can be premature.** Design thinking's "prototype fast" reflex applied to life questions can encourage shipping a half-formed move (a sabbatical, a quit, a relocation) when sustained reflection would surface that the framing itself is wrong. Several reviewers note the book under-weights the legitimacy of sitting with a question for months before acting.
- **The "hidden job market" claim is folklore-grade.** The 80% number Burnett & Evans cite has been traced in multiple debunking pieces to a 1970s outplacement-industry brochure; modern labor-economics data does not support it as a precise figure, though the underlying point (referrals matter) is well-established.
- **Counter-readings.** Read alongside Cal Newport, *So Good They Can't Ignore You* (skill-first, energy follows competence); David Epstein, *Range* (match quality through sampling, not prototyping); Barbara Ehrenreich, *Bright-Sided* (critique of American self-design optimism). All three sharpen what Designing Your Life under-handles.

## Common reader use cases

1. **Mid-career stuck** (30–50): "I've been doing X for 12 years, the energy is gone, I don't know what's next." The Odyssey Plans force the question into a concrete three-path frame instead of an abstract one.
2. **Late-twenties direction-seeking**: someone with the Stanford-adjacent profile the book was built for — graduate-school choice, first or second job pivot, geographic move. This is the book's home turf.
3. **Recent retirees and empty-nesters** asking "what now?" — the Life Design programs explicitly market here, and the toolkit (Compass, Wayfinding, Odyssey Plans) maps better to "rebuild from blank" than to "incremental career move."
4. **Career coaches and HR practitioners** looking for a structured framework to use with clients — the book is widely taught in coaching certifications because the exercises are facilitator-friendly.
5. **University career-services offices** — Life Design Studio for Educators has trained staff at 300+ institutions; many undergraduate career centers run Odyssey Plan workshops as standard programming.
6. **Couples / partners doing joint planning** — the Compass + Odyssey Plans format works as a conversation scaffold (Life Three for both partners surfaces value tensions cheaply).

## Adjacent books

- **Cal Newport, *So Good They Can't Ignore You* (2012)** — direct counter-frame on the energy/passion question. Newport says skill precedes love; Designing Your Life says energy-tracking is the compass. Read both; the answer is closer to Newport on direction-setting and closer to Burnett & Evans on tactics-once-direction-is-set.
- **David Epstein, *Range* (2019)** — overlapping prescription (sample widely, optimize for match quality) but grounded in research rather than design-thinking heuristics.
- **Daniel Pink, *Drive* (2009)** — the SDT (autonomy/mastery/purpose) substrate Designing Your Life gestures at without citing.
- **Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, *Flow* (1990)** — the actual psychological research behind the Wayfinding journal's engagement metric.
- **Sheena Iyengar, *The Art of Choosing* (2010)** — the choice-overload research Burnett & Evans cite in Ch. 9.
- **Barbara Ehrenreich, *Bright-Sided* (2009)** — the strongest counter-frame: American self-design optimism is itself a class artifact and ideology.
- **Herminia Ibarra, *Working Identity* (2003)** — academic predecessor on career transitions through small experiments and identity prototyping, with more empirical grounding (qualitative longitudinal study of career changers) and less Stanford polish.
- **Bill Burnett & Dave Evans, *Designing Your Work Life* (2020)** — the in-job sequel; smaller in scope, focused on redesigning a job you can't leave.
