## Core methodology

The book is a popularization of ~four decades of cognitive-psychology research on memory, mostly from the Roediger lab, the McDaniel lab, and the Bjorks' UCLA lab. Brown supplies the chapter-opening narratives (a Marine officer learning parachute operations, a neurosurgeon mid-crisis, a Minnesota highway-patrol trainee). Eight chapters, ~12 named concepts:

- **Chapter 1, Learning Is Misunderstood**. Three claims: (a) learning is deeper when effortful; (b) we are poor judges of when we are learning; (c) re-reading and massed practice are the dominant student strategies and the least effective ones. Frames the book as overturning everyday intuition with lab evidence.
- **Chapter 2, To Learn, Retrieve**. The **testing effect / retrieval practice**. Foundational citation: Roediger & Karpicke (2006), "Test-Enhanced Learning: Taking Memory Tests Improves Long-Term Retention," *Psychological Science* 17, 249-255 — students who studied a passage then took a free-recall test outperformed students who restudied on a one-week delayed test (~50% vs ~40% recall), with the effect growing across longer delays. Roediger & Karpicke (2006), "The Power of Testing Memory" in *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, is the omnibus review.
- **Chapter 3, Mix Up Your Practice**. **Interleaving** (rather than blocked practice) and **variation in practice conditions**. Cites Rohrer & Taylor (2007) on math problems, Kornell & Bjork (2008) on painting styles. The book's example: medical residents trained on interleaved arterial-line placements outperform those trained on blocked sets, though the interleaved group *feels* worse during practice.
- **Chapter 4, Embrace Difficulties**. The **desirable difficulties** frame from Robert A. Bjork (1994). Spacing, interleaving, varied conditions, generation, and contextual interference are all "difficulties" that hurt short-term performance and help long-term retention. The book is explicit that the source concept is Bjork's, not theirs.
- **Chapter 5, Avoid Illusions of Knowing**. **Fluency illusions** and metacognitive miscalibration. Cites Koriat & Bjork on judgment of learning, Dunning-Kruger, the Mia Blundetto Marine parachute story for "you don't know what you don't know." Re-reading produces familiarity, which the learner mistakes for mastery; **familiarity ≠ retrievability**.
- **Chapter 6, Get Beyond Learning Styles**. Rebuts the VAK / Kolb / Honey-Mumford learning-styles industry, leaning on **Pashler, McDaniel, Rohrer & Bjork (2008)**, *Psychological Science in the Public Interest* 9(3): no evidence that matching instruction to a learner's stated style improves outcomes. Note: McDaniel is a co-author on the paper being summarized here — this chapter is closest to the book's primary research.
- **Chapter 7, Increase Your Abilities**. The IQ-as-fixed view is wrong; **structure-building** (Gernsbacher), **rule-learning vs example-learning** (Hunt & Ellis), **growth-mindset** (Dweck) tie-ins. Lower-tier scientific rigor here — much of the chapter is biographical (Bonnie Blair, James Paterson, the Aoki golf-instruction story).
- **Chapter 8, Make It Stick**. Practical advice for students, teachers, trainers, lifelong learners. Practitioner-oriented; the most prescriptive chapter. Concrete techniques: low-stakes weekly quizzes, deliberate generation before reading, spacing intervals roughly tracking the forgetting curve, interleaving across topics within a study session, peer-teaching as forced retrieval.

**~14 indexable concepts**: testing effect (Ch. 2); generation effect (Ch. 2); elaboration (Ch. 2, 7); spacing effect (Ch. 4); interleaved practice (Ch. 3); varied practice (Ch. 3); desirable difficulties (Ch. 4); reflection as covert retrieval (Ch. 2); fluency illusion (Ch. 5); calibration / metacognition (Ch. 5); the learning-styles myth (Ch. 6); mnemonic devices — memory palace, peg system (Ch. 8); structure-building (Ch. 7); rule- vs example-learners (Ch. 7).

**What the book *aggregates* vs *invents***. It invents nothing. It is a high-quality synthesis of: Roediger lab (testing effect, retrieval-based learning), McDaniel lab (prospective memory, retrieval, learning-styles critique), Bjork lab (desirable difficulties, fluency, spacing), Karpicke (testing effect in classrooms), Pashler (learning styles), Dunlosky (effective study strategies — Dunlosky et al. 2013 *PSPI* is the companion review). The book's contribution is *translation* — turning peer-reviewed effect sizes into actionable instruction.

## Critique / honesty

**This is one of the most empirically defensible books in the slate.** The core claims survive meta-analysis. The critique cannot be manufactured. But honest scope still has named, sourced tensions.

- **Testing effect is robust but the effect size in real classrooms is smaller than the book implies.** Adesope, Trevisan & Sundararajan (2017), *Review of Educational Research* 87(3) — meta-analysis of 217 testing-effect studies, g ≈ 0.61-0.70. Rowland (2014) meta-analysis: g ≈ 0.50. Yang et al. (2021) classroom-only meta: g ≈ 0.50. These are real medium-to-large effects, but readers of *Make It Stick* often infer effects closer to Roediger & Karpicke's original ~10-percentage-point gap on a single-passage lab study. In a full course, the marginal effect of one more quiz on a final grade is real and modest, not transformational.
- **Boundary conditions the book under-emphasizes.** Van Gog & Sweller (2015) argued the testing effect attenuates for complex, high-element-interactivity materials. Karpicke & Aue (2015) rebutted this in *Educational Psychology Review* 27 — the effect holds for complex materials too — but the debate is real, and the book mostly side-steps it. Brunmair & Richter (2019), *Psychological Bulletin* — meta-analysis of 59 interleaving studies, overall g = 0.42, but the effect was strongest for paintings (g = 0.67), moderate for math (g = 0.34), small-non-significant for expository text (g = 0.21), and *negative* for word lists (g = -0.39). Interleaving is not a universal recommendation; it depends on category similarity and material type.
- **Retrieval-induced forgetting.** Benjamin Storm and others have documented that practicing retrieval of subset A from a study set causes selective forgetting of related but unretrieved subset B — across delays of up to a week (Storm et al. on RIF). The book mentions this only briefly. For curriculum designers who quiz on Topic 1 while assuming Topic 2 is "still in there," this is a real concern.
- **Learning-styles rebuttal is correct but the public take-away is sometimes over-extended.** Pashler et al. (2008) and Willingham et al. (2015) — no evidence that matching instruction to stated style helps. But Willingham himself is careful: this is a critique of *learning styles as a psychological theory*, not a claim that "no individual differences matter for instruction." Students do differ in prior knowledge, working-memory capacity, motivation, language background, and disability status. The book correctly demolishes VAK, but a careless reader can take this as "everyone learns the same way" — which is not what the science says. McDaniel's own work distinguishes rule-learners from example-learners (Ch. 7), which is an individual-difference claim.
- **"Desirable difficulties" can be weaponized to justify gratuitous classroom difficulty.** Bjork's original concept is precise: a difficulty is desirable only if it produces *productive struggle* — the learner has the prerequisite knowledge and strategies to engage with it. The **expertise-reversal effect** (Kalyuga, Ayres, Chandler & Sweller) shows that techniques effective for novices can become unhelpful or harmful for experts, and vice versa. Teachers sometimes invoke "desirable difficulties" to defend confusing problem sets that produce frustration without productive struggle. The book mentions this risk but does not arm readers to detect it.
- **Application to ADHD, dyscalculia, severe anxiety, language disorders is under-discussed.** Retrieval practice does benefit these populations (research on developmental language disorders, dyslexia, ADHD shows comparable testing-effect magnitudes), but the book frames difficulty as a universal good. Readers with mental-health-related learning blocks often misapply: they push harder difficulty, hit overload, conclude they are "incapable." The block is not the technique. The book does not flag this user.
- **Working-memory interaction is nuanced.** Agarwal & Finley (2014) — retrieval practice with feedback benefits lower-working-memory students *more*. But Buchin & Mulligan (2023) — retrieval practice can be net-costly when working-memory load is high and the material is complex. The book treats retrieval as a free win; recent literature shows trade-offs.
- **The "feeling of fluency ≠ learning" message can be over-extended.** Bjork lab evidence is solid: feelings of fluency during study correlate poorly with later recall. But the popular take-away ("if it feels productive, it isn't") is rhetorical, not literal. Some kinds of productive learning *do* feel productive — focused practice on the right difficulty, explanation that lands, working through an example carefully. Treating discomfort as a quality signal is a misread.
- **The book is journalistic synthesis, not original research.** Brown is a novelist; he wrote the prose. Roediger and McDaniel are working scientists, but the book is a popularization. When labs disagree (e.g., interleaving for text materials, retrieval costs under cognitive load) the book tends not to surface the dispute.
- **No replication-crisis caveat.** The book pre-dates the open-science meta-analyses (2017-2024) that tightened effect-size estimates. Most of its claims hold up; readers should know which claims are still moving.

## Common reader use cases

1. **Students preparing for a hard exam (medical boards, bar, qualifying, certification)** who have been re-reading and highlighting for months and feel stuck. They need the retrieval/spacing/interleaving prescription concretely.
2. **Teachers / instructors redesigning a course** to add low-stakes quizzes, retrieval starters, interleaved problem sets — and wanting research backing to defend the design to skeptical colleagues, students, or administrators.
3. **Adult learners picking up a new language, instrument, or technical skill** in their 30s-50s and confused why duolingo-style ease isn't producing durable skill.
4. **Parents of school-age kids** told their kid is a "visual learner" or "kinesthetic learner" and wondering whether to accommodate.
5. **Corporate L&D / training designers** moving away from "watch-and-recall" e-learning toward retrieval-based microlearning and spaced reinforcement.
6. **People recovering from bad study habits** — cramming, highlighting, passive re-reading — who feel their memory is "bad" when really their methods are.
7. **Coaches, music teachers, sports trainers** rethinking block-practice drills toward varied, interleaved practice.

## Adjacent books

- **Carol Dweck, *Mindset* (2006)** — belief substrate beneath effortful learning. *Make It Stick* gives the *techniques* growth mindset effort should deploy; *Mindset* gives the *stance*. Read together.
- **Anders Ericsson & Robert Pool, *Peak* (2016)** — deliberate practice. *Peak* is the procedural extension: not just retrieve, but retrieve with feedback at the edge of ability. The two books cite each other's literatures.
- **Daniel Willingham, *Why Don't Students Like School?* (2009)** — cognitive psychologist for teachers. Same evidence base, different framing; Willingham is the more careful sceptic on individual differences and learning styles.
- **Barbara Oakley, *A Mind for Numbers* (2014)** — popular study guide for STEM students built on the same Bjork/Roediger evidence; more practical, less rigorous.
- **John Dunlosky et al. (2013), "Improving Students' Learning With Effective Learning Techniques," *Psychological Science in the Public Interest* 14(1)** — the peer-reviewed companion. Ranks ten study techniques; only practice testing and distributed practice get a "high utility" rating. If readers can handle one academic paper, this is the one.
- **Pooja Agarwal & Patrice Bain, *Powerful Teaching* (2019)** — teacher-facing operationalization of *Make It Stick* with classroom routines. Agarwal is a Roediger student.
- **David Epstein, *Range* (2019)** — overlaps on desirable difficulties; *Range* uses the same Bjork-lab evidence to argue for wide sampling.
- **Cal Newport, *Deep Work* (2016)** — adjacent on focused-effort learning; complementary.
- **Joshua Foer, *Moonwalking with Einstein* (2011)** — narrative account of memory-palace mnemonics; the experiential companion to *Make It Stick*'s Ch. 8.
