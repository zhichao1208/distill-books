## Core methodology

The book is organized as three parts mapped to a 7-step Behavior Design process, then a section on bad habits and a section on social/group change. Concepts (chapter/section attribution where traceable):

- **B = MAP** : Behavior happens only when Motivation, Ability, and Prompt converge in the same moment. Single equation underneath everything. : Ch. 1 (The Fogg Behavior Model).
- **Three types of prompts** : Person prompts (internal: "I should..."), Context prompts (environmental: sticky note, alarm), Action prompts ("anchors": piggyback on an existing routine). Fogg argues action prompts are the most reliable. : Ch. 1 / Ch. 4.
- **Anchor → Behavior → Celebration (ABC)** : The recipe format. "After I [ANCHOR], I will [BEHAVIOR]. To celebrate, I will [CELEBRATION]." : Ch. 2–6 / Part Two.
- **Tiny Behavior** : The smallest version of the habit that still counts (one push-up, one tooth flossed, one sentence written). Reduces required motivation to near zero so ability alone can carry it. : Ch. 3 (Design / Tiny).
- **Celebration creates "Shine"** : The immediate positive emotion (fist pump, "I did it!", smile) that wires the habit into the basal ganglia via dopamine. Fogg's claim: emotion creates habits, not repetition. Fake celebrations don't work — must feel authentic. : Ch. 4.1 (Practice / Celebration).
- **Emotions create habits, not repetition** : Direct counter to the popular "21 days to a habit" / "66 days" lore. Fogg argues hedonic timing matters more than calendar time. : Ch. 4.
- **Aspirations vs. Outcomes vs. Behaviors** : Aspiration = abstract ("be healthier"); Outcome = measurable ("lose 10 lb"); Behavior = specific action ("walk 20 min after lunch"). Only behaviors can be designed. People confuse the three and try to "do" an aspiration. : Ch. 2 (Selection).
- **Magic Wand Question** : "If I had a magic wand, what behaviors would my person be doing to achieve this aspiration?" Used to generate the Swarm of Bs. : Ch. 2.
- **Swarm of Bs** : Brainstorm of every conceivable behavior that could serve the aspiration. Quantity over quality at this stage. : Ch. 2.
- **Focus Mapping** : Plot each candidate behavior on a 2x2 of impact (will it work?) vs. feasibility (will I actually do it?). Top-right quadrant = Golden Behaviors. : Ch. 2.
- **Golden Behaviors** : The high-impact, high-feasibility behaviors identified by Focus Mapping; the ones worth designing recipes for. : Ch. 2.
- **Ability Chain** : Five links determining how easy a behavior is — Time, Money, Physical effort, Mental effort, Routine fit. The weakest link is the bottleneck; making the behavior tiny means strengthening the weakest link. : Ch. 3.
- **Starter Step vs. Scaled-Back Version** : Two tactics for going tiny. Starter Step = the first physical action of the larger behavior ("put on running shoes"). Scaled-Back Version = a miniature of the whole behavior ("two push-ups"). : Ch. 3.
- **Anchor (matching to existing routine)** : Pick an anchor with matching frequency (daily habit → daily anchor), matching location, and matching theme. Most-common anchor failure = mismatched anchor. : Ch. 4.
- **Pearl Habits** : Use an irritant or annoying stimulus as the anchor for a positive response. "After the dog barks, I'll take a deep breath." Turns frustration into a prompt. : Ch. 4 sidebar.
- **Maui Habit** : Fogg's canonical morning habit — "After my feet touch the floor, I will say 'It's going to be a great day.'" Used as the entry-point exercise in his Tiny Habits 5-day program. : Ch. 4 / Practice.
- **Meanwhile Habits** : Use waiting moments (shower, kettle boiling, elevator) for tiny behaviors. : Ch. 4 sidebar.
- **Celebration / Shine taxonomy** : Fogg lists 100+ celebration techniques in an appendix (physical: fist pump, dance step; verbal: "Yes!", "Boom!"; imagined: crowd cheering). Readers are told to pick what genuinely feels good and switch when it goes stale. : Appendix.
- **Habits grow on their own (multiplication)** : Once a tiny habit is wired in, the behavior naturally expands without effort ("just one push-up" becomes 10). Don't force the expansion; let it emerge. : Ch. 4.2 (Repeat, Refine, Upgrade).
- **Troubleshooting order: Prompt → Ability → Motivation** : When a habit fails, check the prompt first (was the anchor reliable?), then ability (was it small enough?), and only finally motivation. Motivation is the last variable to touch because it is the least reliable. : Ch. 4.2.
- **Three options for unwanted habits** : Create a new substitute habit, stop the old habit directly, or swap the old habit for a new one. Most behavior-change writing only addresses one of the three. : Ch. 5 (Unraveling Bad Habits).
- **General → Specific → Tiny (for stopping)** : To stop a bad habit, break the general habit into specific behaviors, then design tiny disruptions for each. : Ch. 5.
- **Behavior Change Masterplan** : Three phases — Create new habits, Stop unwanted habits, Swap habits — applied sequentially to a life domain. : Ch. 5.
- **Changing With Others** : Ringleader vs. Ninja styles of leading group change. Feedback Power Zones — the intersection of "what matters to the person" and "where they feel insecure"; feedback delivered here lands hardest, for good or harm. : Ch. 6.
- **The Skill of Change** : Behavior change is a meta-skill that improves with practice; each successful tiny habit teaches you about your own design preferences and compounds. : Conclusion / Ch. 7.
- **Habit Recipe Cards / Toolkit** : Physical printable cards for each recipe (Anchor / Behavior / Celebration). Appendix contains 300 sample tiny-habit recipes and 32 ways to "frame for success." : Appendices.

## Common reader use cases

Drawn from the book's own examples, Amazon/Goodreads reviews, and Fogg's public talks:

1. "I want to floss more" → Fogg's canonical recipe: "After I brush my teeth, I will floss one tooth." Single most cited example in reviews.
2. "I want to start a writing habit but never sit down" → "After I pour my morning coffee, I will write one sentence."
3. "I want to do strength training but the gym feels impossible" → "After I use the toilet, I will do two push-ups."
4. "I want to wake up and not immediately doom-scroll" → Maui Habit: "After my feet touch the floor, I will say 'It's going to be a great day.'"
5. "I keep meaning to meditate but never start" → "After I sit at my desk, I will take three deep breaths."
6. "I want to drink more water" → "After I refill my coffee, I will drink one glass of water."
7. "I want to stop checking my phone in bed" → Substitute-and-anchor: "After I plug in my phone in the kitchen, I will pick up the book on my nightstand."
8. "I want to be more affectionate / express more gratitude" → "After I get into bed, I will tell my partner one thing I appreciated today."
9. CEOs designing corporate wellness rollouts that don't depend on willpower (cited in book and Fogg's coaching practice).
10. Physical-therapy and post-injury rehab adherence — readers report sticking to PT exercises by attaching them to morning routines and celebrating completion.

## Adjacent books

- **Atomic Habits — James Clear, 2018.** Most-cited competitor. Clear's "Four Laws" (cue, craving, response, reward) and "two-minute rule" cover the same ground as Fogg's MAP + tiny behavior. Differences widely noted: Clear is identity-first ("who do you want to become"); Fogg is emotion-first ("what will make you feel successful right now"). Clear treats motivation as something to engineer (temptation bundling, motivation rituals); Fogg treats motivation as the last resort behind prompt and ability. Clear is a smorgasbord of tactics; Fogg is a single unified method. Common verdict in side-by-side reviews: Fogg is better for *starting*, Clear is better for *sustaining and scaling*.
- **The Power of Habit — Charles Duhigg, 2012.** Older, journalism-driven. Introduced the popular "cue → routine → reward" loop and case studies (Pepsodent, Alcoa, Target). More descriptive (how habits work) than prescriptive (how to install one). Fogg's contribution vs. Duhigg: a specific design method with a formula and a celebration step, where Duhigg primarily offered the loop and stories.
- **Persuasive Technology: Using Computers to Change What We Think and Do — BJ Fogg, 2002.** Fogg's earlier academic-trade book that introduced "captology" and the precursor to the Behavior Model. Tiny Habits is the consumer crystallization of two decades of that research line.
- **Hooked — Nir Eyal, 2014.** Built on Fogg's model (Eyal was a Fogg student) for product-design audiences (Hook Model: trigger → action → variable reward → investment). Not a self-help book but lives in the same conceptual family; the "trigger" maps to Fogg's prompt.
- **Mini Habits — Stephen Guise, 2013.** Closest competitor in the "small step" subspace. Predates Tiny Habits, advocates one-push-up-style minimums, but lacks the systematic 7-step design process and the celebration mechanism.
- **The Compound Effect — Darren Hardy, 2010.** Adjacent in the "small actions, big results" rhetoric, but more motivational than methodological.
- **Switch — Chip & Dan Heath, 2010.** Behavior change at the organizational level; "Rider / Elephant / Path" frame. Overlaps with Fogg's argument that you must make the path easy, not just motivate the elephant.
