# Research package: Grit

*Grit: The Power of Passion and Perseverance* — Angela Duckworth, Scribner (Simon & Schuster), May 3 2016. 277 pages.

## Facts

- **#1 New York Times bestseller** on release in May 2016; remained on NYT non-fiction lists for over a year.
- **5M+ copies sold worldwide** per Simon & Schuster / author site marketing copy; translated into 40+ languages.
- **TED talk** ("Grit: The power of passion and perseverance," TED Talks Education 2013) is one of the most-viewed TED talks of all time — 29M+ views on TED.com plus YouTube. The talk preceded and seeded the book.
- **Goodreads**: 4.08 / 5 across ~145,400 ratings and ~10,400 reviews. Distribution: 39% five-star, 37% four-star, 17% three-star, 4% two-star, 1% one-star.
- **Author background**: Angela Duckworth (b. 1970) is the Rosa Lee and Egbert Chang Professor at the University of Pennsylvania, founder of Character Lab, and faculty co-director of the Penn-Wharton Behavior Change for Good Initiative. BA Harvard (Neurobiology, magna cum laude); MSc Oxford (Neuroscience, Marshall Scholar); PhD Penn (Psychology, NSF Fellow). Career path: McKinsey consultant → math and science teacher in NYC / SF / Philadelphia public schools → PhD under Martin Seligman.
- **MacArthur Fellow 2013** ("Genius Grant"); APS Rising Star; AAAS Fellow.
- The Grit Scale (12-item original, 8-item Grit-S short form) was first published in Duckworth, Peterson, Matthews & Kelly (2007), *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*.

## Core methodology

Grit is defined as **sustained passion plus perseverance for very long-term goals** — goals measured in years to decades, not weeks. The book bundles roughly fifteen concepts, mostly drawn from Duckworth's own published research, organized in three parts.

- **Grit = Passion + Perseverance** (Ch. 1, "Showing Up"). Duckworth's two-factor construct. Passion is *consistency of interest over time*, not intensity; perseverance is *staying power through setbacks*. The book's central exhibit is the West Point Beast Barracks study (Duckworth et al. 2007).
- **Talent ≠ achievement** (Ch. 2, "Distracted by Talent"). Society over-weights innate talent because effort is invisible. Duckworth's anti-naturalism is the rhetorical engine.
- **The two equations** (Ch. 3, "Effort Counts Twice"). *Talent × effort = skill. Skill × effort = achievement.* Effort enters twice, so its compound contribution exceeds talent's. This is Duckworth's most-quoted formulation; she presents it as a heuristic, not a measured relationship.
- **The Grit Scale** (Ch. 4, "How Gritty Are You?"). 10-item self-report; scores range 1-5. Two subscales: Consistency of Interest (5 items) and Perseverance of Effort (5 items).
- **West Point Beast Barracks** (Chs. 1, 4). 2,441 cadets across two entering classes; one-SD higher grit → 60% higher odds of completing the seven-week induction. Replicated in two later cohorts.
- **National Spelling Bee** (Chs. 1, 7). Duckworth, Kirby, Tsukayama, Bernstein & Ericsson (2011) in *Social Psychological and Personality Science*. Grittier spellers practice more, and **deliberate practice alone in a room** (not quiz-style or reading) fully mediates grit's effect on advancement.
- **Grit grows with age** (Ch. 5). Cross-sectional Grit Scale data show older cohorts score higher; Duckworth treats this as evidence grit is cultivable, though she acknowledges the cohort-vs-development confound.
- **Four assets of growing grit from the inside** (Chs. 6-9): **Interest** (passion is discovered then deepened, not found whole), **Practice** (deliberate practice in Ericsson's strict sense — stretch goal, full focus, immediate feedback, repeat), **Purpose** (connection to something beyond self), **Hope** (growth-mindset-style belief that effort matters, explicitly built on Dweck).
- **Hard Thing Rule** (Ch. 10, "Parenting for Grit"). Family rule Duckworth uses: everyone (including parents) must be doing one hard thing requiring deliberate practice; you can quit only at a natural stopping point (end of season, term), not in the middle; you choose the hard thing.
- **Playing fields of grit** (Ch. 11). Extracurriculars with sustained difficulty — sports, music, debate — correlate with later grit and outcomes. Drawn from Margo Gardner et al.'s longitudinal work on activity participation.
- **Culture of grit** (Ch. 12). Pete Carroll's Seahawks, Jamie Dimon's JPM, KIPP schools, Finland's *sisu*, the Marines. Mostly journalistic case studies.
- **Wee-grit / Big-grit distinction**. Wee-grit = finishing what you start this week; Big-grit = decade-long top-level goal that organizes the rest.

## Critique / honesty

Grit is famous *and* the empirical case for the book's headline claims is substantially weaker than the popular framing implies. This section is heavier than most because the gap between popular reception and evidence is unusually wide.

**Credé, Tynan & Harms (2017), *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology***, "Much Ado About Grit: A Meta-Analytic Synthesis of the Grit Literature." 584 effect sizes from 88 independent samples, n = 66,807. Headline findings:

- **Grit's correlation with performance is modest.** Corrected r ≈ 0.18 with academic performance overall. This is smaller than the popular framing suggests and is dwarfed by cognitive ability (r ≈ 0.50+) and SES.
- **The two-factor structure is not supported.** The Perseverance-of-Effort subscale carries essentially all of grit's predictive power; Consistency-of-Interest's correlation with performance is near zero (r ≈ 0.06). The "passion" half of "passion + perseverance" is empirically weak.
- **Grit is barely distinguishable from conscientiousness.** Meta-analytic ρ between grit and the Big Five conscientiousness facet ≈ 0.84 (across 22 studies). Credé concludes grit is a "jangle fallacy" — a relabeling of an existing construct.
- **Intervention claim**: based on the meta-analysis, grit interventions "may only have weak effects on performance and success."

**Duckworth's own response** (NPR, May 25 2016; later writings). She conceded several points: (a) "I would prefer to think of grit as a member of the conscientiousness family" with possibly independent predictive power; (b) the Consistency-of-Interest subscale is weaker than Perseverance; (c) Grit Scale is correlational, not causal; (d) she publicly bowed out of the **California CORE Districts** plan to use the Grit Scale in K-12 school accountability ratings, telling NPR (May 13 2015) "I do not think we should be doing this; it is a bad idea" because the scale was not built for high-stakes use.

**Range restriction at West Point.** West Point cadets are already an extreme high-grit, high-achievement self-selected sample — they passed the Congressional nomination + physical + academic gauntlet to get in. The Beast Barracks survival rate is **95% overall**; the gritty cadets survive at 98%. The headline-friendly "60% odds ratio" sits on top of a three-percentage-point absolute difference in a population that has already been filtered for the trait. Replicating the prediction *outside* that filter — predicting who becomes a cadet at all, or who succeeds in the general population — is a different and unsolved problem.

**Range (David Epstein, 2019) critique.** Range explicitly pushes back on Duckworth: her Scripps Spelling Bee and West Point samples select on people who already self-selected into the domain. The much larger pool who tried the same activity and quit is invisible. Epstein argues **strategic quitting** to improve **match quality** is how high performers actually navigate winding careers; "never quit" misreads what gritty winners did, because the winners are the ones who happened to land on a good match.

**Alfie Kohn critique** (Washington Post April 6 2014; Independent School magazine Fall 2014; *The Myth of the Spoiled Child*). Four threads:

1. **Victim-blaming and structural avoidance.** Asking poor children to be grittier shifts attention from healthcare, housing, school funding, segregation. Kohn calls out Duckworth's stated preference for character education over expanded Medicaid as a political claim disguised as a psychological one.
2. **Conformism, not creativity.** High-grit students correlate with high-conformity behavior; rewarding grit selects for compliance with imposed goals.
3. **Goals are not the child's.** "Hard things" in the Grit framework are usually adult-chosen — finish the violin lessons, finish the season — not goals the child generated.
4. **Knowing when to quit** is a real cognitive skill the grit frame obscures. Nonproductive persistence is a documented failure mode.

**"Follow your passion" tension.** The interest chapters lean on the discover-and-deepen model — passion comes from sustained engagement, not innate calling. But the book's marketing and pop reception read as "find your one big passion and stick with it," which contradicts Cal Newport's *So Good They Can't Ignore You* (2012): passion is the *output* of competence, not the input. The book is closer to Newport than its reception suggests; readers often miss this.

**Grit ≠ Mindset.** Duckworth and Dweck are sometimes conflated. Mindset is a belief about ability malleability; Grit is a behavioral disposition. Duckworth herself names growth mindset as one route to grit (Ch. 9). The replication critique applies to both, in different forms — Mindset has small intervention effects (Sisk et al. 2018, d ≈ 0.08); Grit has small performance correlations (Credé 2017, r ≈ 0.18) and weak construct distinctness.

**Where the book holds up best.** The Hard Thing Rule as parenting practice; the deliberate-practice chapter (which is essentially Ericsson translated for parents); the discover-and-deepen model of interest. The framework's core intuition — long-term goals require sustained, structured practice with periodic recommitment — survives the methodological critique. What does not survive is the claim that the Grit Scale measures something distinct from conscientiousness or that grit alone predicts success at the magnitudes the popular framing implies.

## Common reader use cases

1. **Parents debating whether to let a child quit** an instrument, sport, or activity mid-season. The Hard Thing Rule gives them a non-arbitrary heuristic.
2. **Mid-career professionals questioning long-term commitment** to a field — should I keep going, am I just grinding for grinding's sake.
3. **Teachers and coaches** building a culture around effort and deliberate practice rather than visible talent.
4. **Athletes / musicians / makers in plateau phase** where progress has stopped feeling rewarding and the question is "is more grit the answer."
5. **People recovering from a "talent" identity** — being told they were gifted, then stalling — and looking for a different self-story.
6. **Founders / leaders** rationalizing endurance through a multi-year build that hasn't paid off yet.
7. **Researchers / students** deciding whether to read the book itself or just the meta-analysis and Range critique.

## Adjacent books

- **Carol S. Dweck, *Mindset* (2006)** — the belief substrate Duckworth treats as one route to grit (Ch. 9). Same replication-critique architecture.
- **David Epstein, *Range* (2019)** — direct counter on quitting and match quality; sampling-period argument against Duckworth's selection-on-success.
- **Cal Newport, *So Good They Can't Ignore You* (2012)** — passion-as-output, not input; partly compatible with Grit's deepen-interest model, sharply opposed to the popular "find your passion and stick" reading.
- **Anders Ericsson & Robert Pool, *Peak* (2016)** — the deliberate-practice research Duckworth leans on in Ch. 7, more rigorously stated.
- **Daniel Pink, *Drive* (2009)** — mastery/autonomy/purpose as the motivation substrate; overlaps with Duckworth's purpose chapter.
- **Mel Robbins, *The Let Them Theory* (2024)** — adjacent on parenting and autonomy; Robbins explicitly argues against the Duckworth-style push-through-resistance frame for children's choices.
- **Paul Tough, *How Children Succeed* (2012)** — popularized Duckworth's research in the K-12 character-education context; reads Grit through schools.
- **Walter Mischel, *The Marshmallow Test* (2014)** — adjacent on self-control / delayed gratification; Duckworth treats self-control as cousin to grit.
- **Daniel Chambliss, "The Mundanity of Excellence"** (1989, sociological essay on elite swimmers) — older direct ancestor of the deliberate-practice and Grit theses.
