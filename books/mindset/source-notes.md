## CRITICAL: The empirical critique (load-bearing for the skill's honest stance)

Mindset is famous AND the empirical case is weaker than the popular framing implies. The skill MUST handle both. The over-claim exit (B6 in boot) is the most important novel design in this skill — it openly tells users when their request exceeds what the evidence supports.

### Key papers / studies

**Sisk, Burgoyne, Sun, Butler, Macnamara (2018) — *Psychological Science***
- Two meta-analyses
- Meta-analysis 1: 273 studies, 365,000+ participants on correlation between mindset and achievement → weak correlation
- Meta-analysis 2: 43 intervention studies, 57,000+ participants
- **Pooled effect of growth-mindset interventions on academic achievement: d = 0.08** (95% CI 0.02-0.14)
- This is roughly one-sixth of the effect size implied by popular accounts
- ~one-third of intervention studies didn't even verify that students' mindsets had shifted (i.e., the manipulation check failed)

**Macnamara & Burgoyne (2022)** — updated systematic review
- Same conclusions: most studies low-quality, effects small to null
- When high-quality methodological filters applied (active control, pre-registration, no researcher allegiance) effects often disappeared

**Yeager et al. (2019) — *Nature* — the National Study of Learning Mindsets**
- The largest, most methodologically careful test
- 12,000-16,000 9th-graders across 76 US public high schools
- Two 25-minute online sessions
- Pre-registered, independently analyzed, blinded Bayesian replication
- **Result: +0.10 GPA points in core courses for lower-achieving students** (below median in their school)
- No GPA improvement for higher achievers (though they were more likely to take advanced math)
- **Effect required the school context to be supportive** (peer norms valuing challenge)
- In hostile contexts the intervention did nothing or backfired

### What the data actually supports

| Claim | Evidence |
|---|---|
| The conceptual distinction (fixed vs growth as belief patterns that affect interpretation of effort/failure/criticism) | Strong |
| Process-praise vs person-praise affects subsequent task choice + persistence in kids (Mueller-Dweck 1998) | Replicated, holds up |
| "Yet" reframe as personal linguistic tool | Modest support; works as one of many cognitive techniques |
| Sustained, contextually-supported intervention helps at-risk students modestly | Yeager 2019: +0.10 GPA |
| Brief mindset intervention reliably moves grades for everyone | **Not supported** — Sisk 2018: d=0.08 |
| Workshop / poster / mantra changes culture or grades | **Not supported** |
| Telling someone to "have growth mindset" fixes their performance | **Not supported** — Dweck herself calls this "false growth mindset" |

### Dweck's own clarifications

Dweck has publicly clarified what growth mindset means in response to widespread misapplication:

- **2015 *Education Week*, "Growth Mindset, Revisited"**: clarified that the original book over-emphasized sheer effort; strategy + asking for help + persistence on what actually matters are essential
- **2016 KQED MindShift interview**: people are mixtures of fixed and growth across domains, not categorically one or the other
- **2017 *Education Week*, "Misinterpreting the Growth Mindset"**: "false growth mindset" — slapping the label on what's still fixed-mindset praise — is now widespread; growth mindset cannot mean "tell kids any effort is great"

These clarifications inform when-effort-isnt-working.md scene specifically.

### Where the book is empirically weakest

- **Business chapter (Enron, Iacocca)**: journalistic narrative, not evidence
- **Relationships chapter**: generalizes from a handful of studies
- **Sports chapter**: relies heavily on biography
- **School-achievement chapter**: most evidence-backed but most undercut by post-2018 replication literature

### Where the book holds up best

- **The conceptual distinction itself** (fixed vs growth as belief patterns)
- **The process-praise studies** (Mueller-Dweck 1998 has replicated)
- **The "yet" reframe as a practical linguistic tool**
- **The catalog of fixed-mindset internal monologue** and how to recognize it

These are useful even if the intervention effect on test scores is modest. The skill uses these well-supported tools and explicitly avoids the over-claimed applications.

## Chinese edition notes

The Chinese title 《终身成长》 (literally "lifelong growth") is a marketing-strong choice that potentially over-promises in the same direction as the English popular framing. The Chinese-language self-help market has run with this even more enthusiastically than the English market.

Key terminology:
| English | Chinese |
|---|---|
| Growth mindset | 成长型思维 |
| Fixed mindset | 固定型思维 |
| Yet | "yet" (often kept in English in Chinese editions because no clean equivalent — sometimes 还 / 还没 / 暂时还 used) |
| Process praise | 过程表扬 / 努力表扬 |
| Person praise | 人物表扬 / 天赋表扬 |
| False growth mindset | 假性成长思维 / 错误成长思维 (translations vary) |
| Implicit theories of intelligence | 内隐智力理论 |

## Related / complementary books

- **Grit** (Angela Duckworth, 2016) — downstream behavior (passion + perseverance); Mindset is the belief substrate that enables grit, but the books have different empirical bases (Grit's predictive validity has also been challenged)
- **Drive** (Daniel Pink, 2009) — motivation source (autonomy, mastery, purpose); different angle, complementary
- **Self-Theories** (Dweck's academic original, 2000) — the technical academic version of Mindset; cited in source notes only
- **Peak** (Anders Ericsson, 2016) — practice method (deliberate practice); answers the "what specifically should the effort look like" question Mindset leaves vague
- **Self-Compassion** (Kristin Neff, 2011) — addresses the harshness of the fixed-mindset voice from a different angle; could be future complementary skill

## Important limitations / counterpoints

- **The book oversold its educational application.** The skill must not repeat this overclaim.

- **"Growth mindset" has become marketing.** The phrase is now everywhere, often used to dismiss real structural problems ("just have growth mindset" replacing real systemic support). Skill is alert to this.

- **The intervention has been weaponized in some contexts** to blame underperforming students or workers for their performance rather than examining structural barriers. The skill never tells a struggling user "you just need growth mindset."

- **Disability / neurodivergence intersection**: "growth mindset" can be used to dismiss real accommodation needs (dyslexia, ADHD, autism). The not_for list and the disability_dismissal_risk branch address this.

- **Cultural variation**: most growth-mindset research is on US white middle-class students. Cross-cultural replication is mixed. Skill doesn't claim universality.
