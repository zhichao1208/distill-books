# Scene: catching fixed-mindset self-talk

## Trigger

User says some version of: "I'm just not good at X" / "I'm not a math person / writer / runner / X-person" / "I can't do this" / after a failure: "this proves I'm not cut out for it."

Routes from main SKILL.md cold-start A.

## The four-step internal-monologue protocol (book Ch. 8)

Dweck's most actionable contribution. Steps in order:

1. **Notice the voice** — recognize when the fixed-mindset voice is talking
2. **Recognize you have a choice** — the voice is one of several possible interpretations, not the truth
3. **Talk back with the growth voice** — specifically the "yet" reframe + strategy-focused alternative
4. **Take growth action** — small concrete thing in the next 24h that the fixed voice would have stopped

## Step 1: Notice the voice — what does it actually sound like?

```
The fixed-mindset voice has a signature. It speaks in terms of:
  - Permanent identity ("I'm a person who...")
  - Categorical judgment ("can't" not "haven't yet")
  - Self as the variable ("I'm just not good at this") rather than 
    strategy as the variable ("this approach isn't working")
  - Future closed ("I'll never..." vs "I haven't found...")

Right now, tell me — what did the voice say to you? Type the exact
words. Not a paraphrase, not what it should have said. The actual
internal sentence.
```

After the user shares the actual sentence, the skill should reflect it back:

```
You said: "[exact words]"

That's the voice. Notice it has the signature — [identify which
markers from above were present].

Step 1 complete. You caught it. That's the first time, today.
```

## Step 2: Recognize you have a choice

```
The voice is ONE interpretation. There are others. Examples for
your exact sentence:

[Generate 2-3 alternative interpretations, e.g. if user said
"I'm just not good at writing":]

  Interpretation 1 (the fixed voice): "I'm not a writer."
  Interpretation 2 (more accurate): "I haven't practiced writing
                                     enough to be good at it yet."
  Interpretation 3 (strategy-focused): "The way I've been writing
                                       isn't working; the approach
                                       needs to change."

The fixed voice is loudest, but it's not more true than the others.
It just got there first.

You don't have to PICK an interpretation. Just notice that there's
more than one available.
```

## Step 3: The "yet" reframe + strategy-focused alternative

```
Take your sentence and do TWO things to it:

  A. Add "yet" at the end. Say it out loud, even alone.
     "I'm not good at writing ... yet."
     
  B. Replace "I'm" with "this approach is."
     "I'm bad at writing." → "This approach to writing isn't working."

Notice which one of A or B feels more right for your specific case.
B is more powerful when the fixed-thinking is about a specific
performance (a project, a meeting, a relationship). A is more
powerful when the fixed-thinking is about an identity ("I'm a
person who").

This step takes 5 seconds when you remember to do it. The book's
honest claim: this won't change anything if you do it once. It
might change something if you do it 20 times over a month, every
time the fixed voice arrives. That's the work.
```

## Step 4: Take growth action — one small concrete thing

The reframe is incomplete without action. The voice will return tomorrow if you don't make a behavioral difference today.

```
What's ONE specific thing in the next 24 hours that the fixed
voice would have stopped you from doing?

Examples paired with the original fixed-voice sentence:

  "I'm bad at writing" → write one paragraph and send it to one
                         person (not for feedback, just to send)
  "I'm not a runner" → put on running shoes and walk to the corner
                       (not run; just walk; the shoes are the action)
  "I'm bad at math" → spend 10 minutes on one problem you've been
                      avoiding, willing to not finish
  "I'm not good at networking" → send one message to one person 
                                 you respect, with one specific
                                 question

The action must be:
  - Small (under 30 minutes)
  - Specific (one identifiable thing, not a category)
  - Done in the next 24h
  - Process, not outcome (you don't have to do it WELL; you have
    to do it AT ALL)

Tell me your one thing.
```

## Closer (type A — concrete linguistic move)

```
Your one thing: [the user's named action]

Next 24h, do it.

When the fixed voice shows up again (it will), notice → choose → 
reframe with "yet" or the "this approach" swap → and remember 
you already proved today that the voice isn't always right.

Come back if you want. Say "did the action / didn't / caught 
the voice X more times / fixed voice changed shape" — any of 
those works. Not coming back is also fine.
```

## Failure cases

- **User can't think of the exact voice sentence** ("I just feel bad about it").
  → That's data. The voice may be diffuse or somatic rather than verbal. Ask: "When the bad feeling comes, what BODY signal arrives first? (Tightness? Heat? Slump?)" Sometimes the verbal voice is downstream of a body signal, and naming the body signal first unlocks the words.

- **User adds "yet" and feels nothing.**
  → Normal first time. The reframe is a behavioral move (something done repeatedly), not a magic spell. The skill should NOT pretend the user should feel transformed. "It's supposed to feel small. The work is doing it again next time the voice shows up."

- **User says "but maybe the voice is right — maybe I really am bad at this."**
  → The book's distinction: the voice may be partly accurate about CURRENT state ("I can't do this RIGHT NOW") while wrong about TRAJECTORY ("I'll never be able to"). The reframe addresses the trajectory claim, not the current-state claim. Acknowledge the current state honestly. "You may genuinely be at a basic level today. The reframe doesn't deny that. It denies that today's level is your final level."

- **User catches the voice but the action feels impossible (e.g., severe avoidance).**
  → Two paths: (1) make the action 10x smaller until it's possible (Tiny Habits territory — consider routing to that skill); (2) if the avoidance is severe (months of total inability to act), this may not be a mindset issue — depression / trauma / clinical avoidance can present this way. Check the vulnerable_depression / vulnerable_trauma signals in main SKILL.md.

## Common voices and their reframes (reference)

| Fixed voice | "Yet" reframe | "Approach" reframe |
|---|---|---|
| "I'm bad at math" | "I'm bad at math yet" | "This way of learning math isn't working" |
| "I'm not a writer" | "I'm not a writer yet" | "The way I'm trying to write isn't working" |
| "I'll never lose weight" | "I haven't found the approach yet" | "What I've tried hasn't worked, that's data" |
| "I'm not good at relationships" | "I haven't built relational skill yet" | "This pattern in my relationships isn't working" |
| "I'm not creative" | "I'm not practiced at creative work yet" | "What I'm trying to make doesn't fit how I work" |
| "I'm bad at public speaking" | "I'm bad at public speaking yet" | "This style of preparation isn't working for me" |
| "I'm just not motivated" | (See Tiny Habits skill — this is a different problem) | (Motivation usually means prompt or ability problem, not mindset) |

## What this scene doesn't do

- Doesn't tell user the fixed voice is "wrong" categorically (it may be partly right about current state)
- Doesn't promise the reframe will feel powerful immediately (it shouldn't, first 20 times)
- Doesn't substitute for trauma / depression therapy if those are the actual problem
- Doesn't recommend mindset content (apps, courses, posters) — the linguistic work is the work
