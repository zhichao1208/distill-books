# Scene: parenting after talent-praise

## Trigger

Parent says some version of:
- "I told my kid 'you're so smart' for years and now she avoids hard things"
- "I've been over-praising and he can't take feedback"
- "My daughter quits the second something gets hard"
- "I want to switch how I talk to my kids about ability"

Most common skill scenario per research. Routes from main SKILL.md cold-start B.

## First: validate the move toward repair

Parents in this scene often arrive with guilt. Address it directly so they can engage with the work rather than spiral.

```
Two things first:

  1. "You're so smart" was the loving move. It was also the standard
     parenting advice for decades. Almost everyone who says this 
     does it from love, often from being praised that way themselves.
     
  2. The realization you're having now — that smart-praise is making
     your kid risk-averse — is the same realization Dweck's research
     made formal. It's not a parenting failure to have used the
     popular wisdom. It IS a parenting strength to update once you
     notice the pattern.

Now we can talk about the switch without the guilt overhead.
```

## The Mueller-Dweck (1998) study — what actually happened

Briefly ground the parent in the evidence before giving them the linguistic switch:

```
The study Dweck's parenting advice is built on:

  Two groups of kids did a puzzle task that they succeeded on.
  
  Group A heard "you must be smart at this."
  Group B heard "you must have worked hard at this."

  Next task: hard puzzles. Group A (smart-praised) chose easier
  puzzles, performed worse, gave up faster, and were more likely
  to lie about their scores. Group B (effort-praised) stayed with
  the hard ones, learned more, and reported scores honestly.

The mechanism: smart-praise creates an identity ("I'm a smart kid")
that the kid then has to PROTECT. Hard tasks threaten the identity.
Failure threatens the identity. So they avoid both.

Effort-praise creates a process ("I work hard"), which doesn't get
threatened by difficulty. Hard tasks become evidence of the process,
not a threat to it.

This part of the book is well-replicated and holds up.
```

## The three categories of process praise (replace person-praise with)

```
What to say instead of "you're so smart":

  STRATEGY — name the specific approach
    "I noticed you tried a different way when the first didn't work."
    "Asking for help when you got stuck was a smart move."
    "Switching the order of steps was a real thinking move."
    
  EFFORT (specifically what kind)
    "You stuck with that even when it got frustrating."
    "You stayed focused on this for a long time."
    "You came back to it after the break — that took something."
    
  PROGRESS — show them their own growth
    "Compare today to two weeks ago — this section was hard then
     and you barely paused on it just now."
    "You used to need help with this part. You did it alone today."
    "The drawings from September to now — look at the difference."

What NOT to say (false growth praise, which Dweck warns about):

  ❌ "You tried so hard!" — said to a kid who didn't actually try.
     Kids see through it. Worse than smart-praise because it adds
     dishonesty to the dynamic.
     
  ❌ "Any effort is good effort." — no, strategic effort builds
     skill. Empty effort (banging head against the wall the same
     way) doesn't. Dweck specifically corrected this post-2015.
     
  ❌ "It doesn't matter if you fail." — kids feel the lie. Failure
     does matter to them; pretending otherwise teaches them you
     don't take their experience seriously.
     
  ❌ "Smart people work hard too." — still anchored on smart as
     the identity. Same trap, longer sentence.
```

## The switch is one-catch-per-day, not an overhaul

Parents who try to overhaul their language all at once fail (and feel worse). The skill should normalize the small-step approach.

```
You can't rewrite years of language in a week. Don't try.

Instead, the one-catch-per-day approach:

  - This week, catch ONE moment per day when you were about to say
    "you're so smart" (or similar person-praise) and SWAP it for
    one process form.
    
  - That's it. One catch. Not all praise. Not perfectly.
    
  - When you catch it, internally celebrate (Tiny Habits style —
    yes, internally; the kid doesn't need to know you swapped).
    
  - The other moments where you slip back to smart-praise — let
    them go. You're not trying to be a perfect mindset parent.
    You're trying to add new language to your repertoire over
    months.

A year of one-catch-per-day adds up to ~250 process-praise moments
your kid has heard from you. That's the volume that shifts the
identity-formation, not 7 days of perfection.
```

## The "talk to your kid about the change" question (optional)

For kids 8+, the parent might consider explaining the shift:

```
For older kids (8+), you can also have one explicit conversation:

"I've been thinking about how I praise you. I notice I've been saying
'you're so smart' a lot, and I think that might have been making
hard things feel scarier — like if you struggled it would mean you
weren't smart anymore. That's not how it works. I'm going to try
to notice the specific things you DID instead of saying 'smart.'
It might feel weird at first. Tell me if you notice it."

Kids appreciate being treated as collaborators in their own development.
This is also a powerful modeling moment: you, the parent, noticing
your own pattern and adjusting. That's growth mindset enacted, not
preached.

For younger kids (under 8): skip the conversation, just shift the
language. They'll absorb it without commentary.
```

## When the kid is already heavily smart-praised (years in)

If the pattern is long and the kid is now actively avoiding hard things, the switch alone might not fix the avoidance. Honest follow-up:

```
If your kid has been smart-praised for 5+ years and is now showing
serious task avoidance (refuses anything that might be hard, melts
down at failure, lies about performance), the language switch is
necessary but might not be sufficient on its own.

Other supports that compound:
  - Tasks where struggle is structurally normal (sports, music,
    arts) and where the kid can see other kids also struggling
  - A trusted teacher / coach who explicitly values process
  - Time. The undoing takes longer than the original setup.
  - In severe cases (school refusal, intense perfectionism causing
    distress) — a child psychologist who works with anxiety can be
    appropriate

The book gives the linguistic tools. Sometimes the parent has done
this work and the kid still needs more support. That's not failure,
that's reading the actual situation honestly.
```

## Closer (type A — one concrete action for the week)

```
This week, ONE thing:

  Tomorrow, in one specific moment, catch yourself about to say
  "you're so smart" / "you're so talented" / "you're so [trait]" 
  and swap it for one of the three process forms.
  
  Don't tell your kid you're doing it (unless they're 8+ and the
  meta-conversation feels right). Just do the switch.
  
  Don't try to do it 10 times. Do it ONCE, intentionally.

When you catch yourself successfully, take a quiet internal
celebration — you just did the work the book is actually about.

Come back in a week if you want, and tell me which moment you
caught and which form you swapped to. Or tell me you forgot.
Both are useful. Not coming back is also fine.
```

## Failure cases

- **Parent comes back saying "I tried but my kid still avoids hard things."**
  → That's one week. Real shifts take months. Skill should normalize: "One week of caught swaps is a real start, not a fix. Keep going. If after 3 months the avoidance pattern is the same, then we look at other supports."

- **Parent says "my partner doesn't agree with this approach and keeps saying 'you're so smart' anyway."**
  → Honest answer: the kid gets the average. If one parent does process-praise and one does smart-praise, the kid integrates both. The skill doesn't try to fix the partner. "You can only control your half. Do your half. Don't make it a partner conflict."

- **Parent says "I tried to explain it to my kid and they got upset."**
  → Common. The explanation can feel like the kid is being told "the way you've been valued was wrong." Walk back: "You don't have to explain. Just shift the language. The shift teaches without naming."

- **Parent reveals their OWN childhood was heavy smart-praise and they're now realizing it shaped them.**
  → Validate without therapizing. "That's real. Many parents reading Mindset have this realization. It's worth its own conversation (with a therapist if it goes deep), but for the parenting question — you can interrupt the pattern for your kid even if no one interrupted it for you. That's already worth something."
