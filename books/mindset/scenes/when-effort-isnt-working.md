# Scene: when effort isn't working

## Trigger

User says: "I've been trying really hard and not getting anywhere" / "I've been at this for months and not improving" / "My teacher said I just need a growth mindset, but I'm working as hard as I can."

This scene handles the **"false growth mindset"** problem that Dweck herself corrected post-2015. The popular reading of the book ("effort always pays off") leads to this exact failure mode. The skill must engage with it honestly.

Routes via state-first when user mentions sustained effort without progress.

## First: name the trap

```
What you described is something Dweck specifically corrected in
2015. The original book over-emphasized SHEER EFFORT — "try harder
and you'll get there." That message, taken alone, leads to exactly
the trap you're in: months of effort, no progress, growing despair.

The 2015 correction (article: "Growth Mindset, Revisited"):

  Growth mindset is NOT "effort always pays off."
  Growth mindset IS "different STRATEGIES + different APPROACHES + 
  asking for help + persistence ON THE THINGS THAT ACTUALLY MOVE 
  THE NEEDLE."

  Empty effort (banging head against the wall the same way for
  months) doesn't build skill. It builds exhaustion and the false
  conclusion "I'm not cut out for this."

You're not failing growth mindset. The popular framing of growth
mindset failed you.
```

## Three diagnostic questions

```
If effort hasn't been working, run these in order:

  1. STRATEGY — have you been doing the same thing repeatedly, or
     have you genuinely tried different approaches?
     
  2. FEEDBACK — do you have a real signal whether what you're doing
     is working, or have you been going on faith?
     
  3. HELP — have you actually asked for help from someone who can
     see what you can't, or have you been trying to figure it out
     alone?

Most users who've been "trying hard and not improving" are weak on
all three. The fix isn't more effort — it's strategy, feedback, and
help, applied to the SAME effort budget you've been spending.
```

## Diagnosis 1: Strategy variation

```
Pick one:

  A. I've been doing roughly the same thing for [weeks/months]
     ("just practice more" / "just keep showing up")
  B. I've tried 2-3 different approaches but none worked
  C. I've tried many approaches and rotated through them quickly

If A → that's the trap. Same input, same output. Time to deliberately
  vary the approach. Find someone who's good at this and ask "what 
  was the SPECIFIC thing that broke through for you?" — the actual 
  strategy, not the cliché.

If B → closer. The next move is FEEDBACK: were the 2-3 approaches
  evaluated properly, or did you give up after a few days each?
  Often "tried 3 approaches" is actually "tried 3 approaches without
  enough reps to see if any was working."

If C → opposite problem. You may be approach-hopping without
  letting any strategy mature. Pick one and commit to it for a
  defined period (4-8 weeks for most skills) before evaluating.
```

## Diagnosis 2: Feedback signal

```
Without feedback, effort runs blind. Critical question:

  When you put in time on this, how do you know if it's working?

Examples of real feedback:
  - Language learning: a tutor who corrects errors in real time
  - Writing: a reader who tells you specifically what landed and
    what confused them
  - Coding: tests that pass/fail, code review from a senior
  - Music: a teacher with a trained ear who can spot the technical
    issue
  - Fitness: measurable performance (numbers, video, a coach watching)
  - Public speaking: video of yourself + an honest viewer

Examples of FAKE feedback (insufficient):
  - "It feels like I'm getting better" (self-perception is unreliable)
  - "I've put in the hours" (time != progress)
  - "Encouragement from people who love me" (kind, not informative)
  - "I haven't gotten worse" (low bar)

If you don't have a real feedback signal, that's the bottleneck.
More effort without feedback = same level, longer.
```

## Diagnosis 3: Help

```
Honest question: have you asked someone who is GOOD at this for
specific help on what's stuck?

"Help" doesn't mean motivation or encouragement. It means:
  - "Watch me do this — what specifically am I doing wrong?"
  - "Here's what I tried and why I think it failed — what would
    you have done?"
  - "What did your breakthrough look like — the actual moment, 
    not the inspirational version?"
  - "Can you show me how YOU do this in real time?"

People who've broken through usually have specific transferable
insight. They almost never give it unless asked precisely.

If you haven't asked: that's a step before any more effort.

The fixed-mindset block on asking for help: "It would prove I'm
not smart / capable / talented." Catch this voice and ignore it.
Asking for help IS the growth move; not asking is the fixed-mindset
move dressed up as independence.
```

## The honest "maybe you're aimed wrong" question

For some users, the answer isn't strategy / feedback / help. It's that the goal itself is wrong for them. The skill must be willing to ask this.

```
There's one more question I have to ask, because the book glosses
over it:

  Is the goal you're effort-ing toward actually YOURS, or did you
  inherit it (parent / partner / cultural expectation / 5-year-old
  version of you who has different priorities now)?

Growth mindset can keep you grinding on a goal that was wrong for
you the whole time. The book doesn't say this clearly, but it's
real: effort applied to the wrong goal doesn't build the right
skill, no matter how much strategy you add.

If you're 30+ and "trying hard to be a [thing]" and have been
failing for years, sometimes the most growth-mindset move is to
ask "is this still the right target?" — and if the answer is "I'm
not sure," that's worth investigating BEFORE adding more effort.

You're allowed to change targets. Not every long effort is meant
to succeed; some are meant to teach you what you actually want.
```

## Closer (type B — letter choice → next step)

```
Based on what you said, which is the most likely fix:

  A. STRATEGY: I've been doing the same thing; need to actively
     vary the approach. Find someone who broke through, ask their
     specific move.
     
  B. FEEDBACK: I don't have a real signal. Need to set one up.
     Pick the right kind for what I'm trying to do.
     
  C. HELP: Haven't actually asked someone good for specific help.
     The block was pride or "shouldn't need to ask."
     
  D. GOAL: Honestly, I'm not sure this goal is still mine.
     Worth pausing the effort and re-examining the target.

Pick a letter. Then for the next week:
  - If A: do one strategy-search conversation with someone good
  - If B: set up one feedback channel (tutor, coach, reader, test)
  - If C: send one specific "watch me do this" request
  - If D: don't effort this week. Sit with the goal question.
    Talk to someone (therapist, friend, mentor) about whether
    the goal still fits.

Come back if you want, say which you picked and what happened.
Not coming back is also fine.
```

## Failure cases

- **User says "but I really love this, the goal IS mine."**
  → Good. Then A / B / C are the real moves. Don't second-guess. "OK, then the goal isn't the issue. Pick A, B, or C."

- **User says "the people I'd ask for help are too busy / I don't have a teacher / there's no one."**
  → Honest: that IS a structural problem more than a mindset problem. Skill should acknowledge: "If genuine access to skilled feedback is the bottleneck, the work shifts to FINDING that access — paid coaching, online communities of practice, courses with feedback components. Sometimes 'I'm trying hard alone' is the actual problem and the answer is 'stop trying to do this alone.'"

- **User says "I tried A/B/C months ago and they didn't help either."**
  → Sit with this. May genuinely be the wrong goal, may be a mismatch between the user's specific neurology and the goal (some people genuinely can't develop perfect pitch; some genuinely can't do high-level math at advanced levels regardless of effort). The skill should honor this: "Some goals have real ceilings for individual people. Growth mindset doesn't mean every person can do every thing at every level. It means SOME growth is possible; it doesn't promise unlimited growth in every domain."

- **User reveals depression-shaped grind ("I've been forcing myself even though I hate it").**
  → Route to vulnerable_depression exit in main SKILL.md. Forced effort against hatred is a depression signal, not a strategy problem.

---

## What this scene doesn't do

- Doesn't tell the user "just try harder"
- Doesn't tell the user "everyone can master anything with growth mindset" (false)
- Doesn't pretend the book's "effort = success" framing is correct (it isn't, Dweck corrected this)
- Doesn't replace real coaching / therapy when the issue is goal misalignment or depression
