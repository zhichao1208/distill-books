## Critique sources used (honest-scope) — heavy

- **Credé, Tynan & Harms (2017). "Much Ado About Grit: A Meta-Analytic Synthesis of the Grit Literature." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology.*** 584 effect sizes from 88 independent samples; n=66,807. ## Concepts NOT used and why

- **The Grit Scale as diagnostic tool for individual users** — the scale is correlational, range-restricted, and Duckworth herself withdrew it from high-stakes uses. The skill mentions it for context; does not deploy it.
- **West Point dramatic "60% odds" number** — sits on 98% vs 95% absolute base rate in an already-self-selected sample. Mention for honest scope, not as evidence.
- **Pete Carroll / Jamie Dimon / KIPP corporate-culture chapters** — journalistic case studies, low evidence weight.
- **Finland "sisu" / Marines anecdotes** — narrative color, not method.
- **"Find your passion" reading** — the book's actual model is discover-and-deepen; the popular framing is wrong.

## Why this book and not adjacent ones

Grit is the mindset/identity archetype member #3 (alongside Mindset and Let Them Theory). It's an honest-scope test case: famous, TED-spotlit, widely adopted in K-12 — and the empirical case is substantially weaker than the popular framing implies. The skill exists to give users the *useful* pieces (Hard Thing Rule, deliberate-practice translation, discover-and-deepen) WITHOUT the overclaims. It also explicitly routes to Range when the user is actually facing a strategic-quit / match-quality question that grit framing would harm.

## Public-content basis

This skill was built from public material: Goodreads, the author's faculty page, MacArthur biography, NPR public interviews where Duckworth made her own concessions, peer-reviewed papers (Credé 2017 freely on PubMed via NIH; Schmidt 2020 abstract), critique articles by Alfie Kohn on his own website, and Range's published counter-argument. No verbatim Duckworth book text beyond ~50 words anywhere.
