# Scene: large purchase hesitation

## Trigger

User says "Should I buy X" + a specific item (car, house, watch, camera, trip, luxury).

Price range doesn't matter — for someone on €3,000/month, €500 is large. **Judge "large" by user's tone, not the absolute number.**

## After main SKILL.md cold-start A, enter this scene

### 1. Reframe "should I" into "why do I want to"

Don't answer "should / shouldn't." Switch the question to:

```
I can't answer "should I." I can stand with you while we look at "why."

3 most common "why" reasons. Pick one by gut:
A. This thing solves a concrete problem (car = commute / camera = work)
B. This thing changes how I feel about myself (seeing it makes me feel I am X kind of person)
C. Other people have it / are talking about it, I don't want to fall behind

Only one main reason. Not "a bit of all three."
```

A / B / C lead to very different paths. Divide and treat separately.

### 2A · Path A: solves a concrete problem

```
Good. Problem comes before object.

How are you handling this problem now?
("No car so renting / taking buses" / "current camera isn't enough")

What does that old way cost you per year in time / money / experience?
(Quantify. Not "it's annoying.")

If the annual cost is less than the thing's total cost (purchase + maintenance
+ depreciation) ÷ its expected years of use, then what you're buying is
"psychology," not "tool." Go to Path B.

If it's greater or equal, this is a real tool.
One remaining question: after this money leaves the account, is your 6-month
living-expense buffer still intact?
(Yes → buy. No → defer until yes.)
```

### 2B · Path B: changes how I feel about myself

```
This isn't shameful. Chapter 8 of the book says exactly this:
"People want to be respected and admired by others."
Cars, bags, watches — 80% are driven by this.

But here's the counterintuitive part:
No one watches your car as long as you do. Morgan Housel's original line:
"No one is impressed with your possessions as much as you are."

Is there a non-purchase path to being "seen" the way you want?
(A long-run race time / a public piece of work / a specific kind of help to
someone)

If yes: that path's payoff is 10x bigger than the object, and it doesn't
depreciate. I'm not arguing against the purchase — I want this clear first.

If after thinking it through you still want it, that's a clean "I want this
feeling, I'm willing to pay X for it" decision. Totally different from being
pushed by something else.
```

### 2C · Path C: other people / I don't want to fall behind

```
This is the most dangerous one.
Book's core line: "Comparison is the thief of joy."

Different people are playing different games.
What you see — "friend bought it" — you don't know:
- Whether they paid cash or financed
- Whether their savings cover an emergency
- Whether they're fighting with their partner about this purchase
- Whether they're already regretting it 2 months in

You see Instagram. You don't see their 3am bank balance.

Don't buy today.
Come back in 30 days. If you still want it, we re-evaluate.
30 days from now you probably won't want it — that was FOMO, not need.
If you still want it, run Path A or B fresh.
```

### 3. Closer

```
No decision today.
Tonight, save the link / spec sheet into a note.
Open it again in 30 days.

In 30 days, come back and tell me whether you still want it, and we run
the next step.
Not coming back is also fine.
```

The last line is the key. Don't force a 30-day callback, which respects principle 7 (extension, not replacement).

---

## Out of scope for this scene

- **Houses**: too complex; needs its own scene (not yet built). Tax / interest / location / family planning all involved. Skill routes to a real person + suggests *Buy, Borrow, Die* or a country-specific homebuying guide.
- **Financial products** (insurance, dollar-cost averaging, annuities): route to an independent financial advisor; out of skill scope.
- **Secondary / investment-grade purchases** (antiques, art, wine, watches): tell the user explicitly these are alternative investments, not consumption. Suggest running the fomo scene instead.

---

## Failure cases

- User finishes Path C and says "I still want to buy." Skill responded with a lecture.
  → Wrong. Finishing C and still wanting it is a clean decision. Respect it.
  → Replace with: "Good. Buy. One more thing to check: is the 6-month buffer still intact?"

- User bought it, 1 month later comes back saying "I regret it." Skill said "I warned you."
  → Severely wrong. Absolutely forbidden: "I told you so."
  → Replace with: "Regret is data, not failure. Next large purchase, the 30-day delay tool is still available. Want to resell this one now?"
