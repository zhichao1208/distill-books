## Why these 4 core lenses (out of 20 chapters)

Only 4 chapters' concepts enter the skill as core lenses:

1. **Wealth is what you don't see** — Reshapes the "wealth = assets" default. Inverted at screen 0.
2. **Room for error** — Strongest single rule against "I did the math and it's exactly enough"
3. **Tail events** — Strongest single rule against FOMO and single-bet thinking
4. **Enough** — Long-term mindset anchor

Equally important concepts (luck/risk / never-interrupt-compounding / freedom) aren't lenses because cold-start can only carry 1-2 lenses at a time — too many overwhelm. They appear in scenes instead.

## Chinese edition note

The Chinese edition translates "wealth is what you don't see" as "财富就是你没看见的那部分," which is more direct than the English.

"Enough" is translated as "足够," but "足够" in Chinese carries a "just getting by" connotation. The skill uses both "enough" and a Chinese explanation, because "足够" alone is too weak.

## Related / complementary books

- **The Millionaire Next Door** (Stanley & Danko, 1996) — Empirical basis for wealth-is-invisible
- **A Random Walk Down Wall Street** (Malkiel, 1973+) — Academic prototype of tail events
- **Your Money or Your Life** (Robin & Dominguez, 1992) — Same spirit as "enough"
- **Die With Zero** (Bill Perkins, 2020) — The inverse lens (against over-saving); future possible counterpart skill

## Important limitations / counterpoints

- **Structural poverty**: skill assumes the user is making "how to handle money" decisions, not "how to obtain minimum survival income"
- **High net worth / wealth manager territory**: skill doesn't give specific allocation; doesn't replace real professional service
- **Behavioral finance vs classical finance**: this is the behavioral school, doesn't discuss EMH / CAPM
