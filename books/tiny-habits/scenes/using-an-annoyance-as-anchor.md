# Scene: Pearl Habit — using an annoyance as anchor

## Trigger

User describes a recurring irritant in their day: a noise, an interruption, a notification, a thought they can't stop, a person's habit that bothers them.

Common phrasings:
- "Every time my phone buzzes I lose focus and it drives me crazy"
- "The dog barks every time the mail comes and I can't work through it"
- "My coworker chews loudly and I can't concentrate"
- "I keep getting that 'I'm a fraud' thought and I can't shake it"

This is Fogg's most underused technique. Routes from main SKILL.md cold-start C.

## The Pearl Habit move

```
Fogg names this a Pearl Habit because an oyster turns an irritant
(a grain of sand) into a pearl. You take the thing that grates on
you and use it AS the anchor for a positive behavior. Every time
the irritant happens, the positive behavior happens too.

Two effects:
  1. The irritant becomes a prompt for something good — its negative
     power drains over weeks because it now triggers a positive action
  2. The positive behavior gets free reps — you no longer have to
     remember to do it; the irritant remembers for you

Examples from the book and Fogg's coaching:
  - Phone notification → 3-second look out the window
  - Dog barks → 1 deep breath, smile at the dog
  - Stuck in traffic → name 3 things you're grateful for
  - Email arrives → roll your shoulders once
  - Negative self-thought ("I'm a fraud") → physically place hand on
    chest, say "noted" out loud, return to task
  - Phone rings (unwanted call) → stand up from your chair before
    answering or declining
```

## Design the Pearl Habit (4 steps)

### Step 1: Name the irritant precisely

Vague irritants don't work as anchors. The irritant must be a specific, repeated, recognizable moment.

```
Examples of well-defined irritants (good anchors):
  - Phone notification banner appears
  - Dog barks
  - Email "new message" sound
  - Slack notification dot
  - Specific co-worker's specific sound (chewing, throat-clearing)
  - Specific thought pattern ("I'm not good enough" with this exact
    phrasing — your habitual self-criticism word)

Examples of poorly-defined irritants (bad anchors):
  - "Stress" (too vague)
  - "When I'm overwhelmed" (state, not moment)
  - "When traffic is bad" (depends on threshold judgment)
  - "When my partner does X" (relational, contextual, not always
    the same moment)

What's your specific irritant? Name it like you'd name a sound effect.
```

### Step 2: Pick a tiny positive response

The behavior must be:
- Tiny enough to do every time without disruption (you don't want to ADD overhead from the irritant)
- Genuinely positive (not just neutral — must lean toward something good for you)
- Possible in the moment the irritant happens (can't require leaving the room)

```
Candidates (pick one that fits YOUR irritant):

  Physical:
    - 1 deep breath
    - Roll shoulders once
    - Smile (yes, alone)
    - Stretch one arm overhead
    - Look out a window for 3 seconds

  Cognitive:
    - Name 1 thing you can see right now that's beautiful
    - Repeat a single phrase ("I'm here") to yourself
    - Notice 1 sound that's pleasant (under the irritant's sound)
    
  Verbal:
    - Whisper "thank you" to whoever made the sound (yes, even the
      annoying coworker — practice for being less reactive)
    - Say "interesting" to yourself when the negative thought arrives
    - Say the actual feeling out loud: "frustrated" — naming defuses

Pick one. Pair with your irritant.
```

### Step 3: The recipe + celebration

Pearl Habit recipe format:

```
After [the irritant happens], I will [positive response].
To celebrate, I will [shine — usually internal here, because the 
irritant happens around other people].
```

Celebration in Pearl Habits is usually internal (a quiet "yes," a
mental fist pump, a slight smile) because the irritant often
happens in public or shared space. Internal celebration still works
per Fogg — must feel real, doesn't have to be loud.

### Step 4: Try for one week, then check

```
For the next week, every time the irritant happens, do the response.
Celebrate (internally is fine).

Don't track. Don't journal. Just do.

Come back in a week and tell me:
  - "The irritant doesn't bug me as much anymore" — Pearl is forming
  - "I keep forgetting" — the irritant isn't as reliable as you 
    thought; pick a different one
  - "The response feels forced" — change the response, not the
    anchor; the irritant is still good signal
  - "I'm using the irritant as an excuse to disengage from work" —
    careful, you've created a procrastination prompt; redesign

Not coming back is also fine.
```

## When Pearl Habits backfire

Two failure modes worth flagging:

### 1. The irritant becomes the *only* prompt to do the positive thing

If you only breathe deeply when your phone buzzes, you may end up checking your phone more to "get more deep breaths." This is the gamification trap.

→ Sign that you've built a meta-habit (checking the irritant) on top of the habit. Add an additional, irritant-free anchor for the same positive behavior (e.g., also breathe deeply after sitting down at desk).

### 2. The positive response becomes avoidance

"Every time my partner says X, I take a deep breath" can subtly become "every time my partner says X, I disengage emotionally." The Pearl was supposed to redirect frustration toward presence, but if the breath becomes a wall, that's worse than the original irritation.

→ Sign: the response should connect you to the moment, not separate you from it. If you feel more checked-out after a few weeks, the response is wrong; pick one that brings you toward, not away.

### 3. Repeated negative thoughts as anchor

If the irritant is a self-critical thought ("I'm a fraud," "I'm bad at this") and the user is in a depressive episode, the constant attention to the thought can deepen it even with positive response attached.

→ Skill should redirect: if user reports the negative-thought anchor is in depression context, route to the vulnerable_anhedonia exit in main SKILL.md. Not a Pearl moment; an actual mental health support moment.

## What this scene doesn't do

- Doesn't promise the irritant will stop happening (it won't; the goal is your relationship to it changes)
- Doesn't suggest removing the source of the irritant as the first move (sometimes you can, but Pearl Habit is for irritants you can't remove — barking dog, phone notifications you need, coworker behaviors)
- Doesn't replace boundary-setting with people. If the irritant is a person's repeated harmful behavior, Pearl Habit is the wrong tool — that's a relational conversation, not a habit design.
