# Scene: when the habit keeps failing

## Trigger

User says: "I tried but it didn't stick" / "I did it for 3 days then stopped" / "I keep failing at X" / "I'm just not motivated."

This is the **highest-value scene** in Tiny Habits. Most users who arrive here are in self-blame mode ("I lack willpower"). The book's main therapeutic contribution: it's almost never willpower, almost always one of three concrete fixable things.

Routes from main SKILL.md cold-start B.

## The non-negotiable order: PROMPT → ABILITY → MOTIVATION

Fogg's troubleshooting order is exactly the reverse of what most people instinct toward. Untreated, users will spiral on "I need more motivation" — which is the LAST thing Fogg's frame would treat.

```
Quick rule before we diagnose:
We check 3 things in this order. Don't skip.

  1. PROMPT — did you see the cue at the right moment?
  2. ABILITY — was the behavior small enough?  
  3. MOTIVATION — last, and rarely the actual problem.

Most people get this backwards and start with "I need more willpower."
That's the diagnostic equivalent of saying "the engine won't start —
maybe I need a better steering wheel." Wrong system.

Let's go in order.
```

## Diagnosis 1 — PROMPT failure (most common)

```
First check: when the habit was supposed to happen, were you reliably
PROMPTED to do it?

Sub-questions:
  - Was the anchor something you actually do every single day, at the
    same physical moment? Or was it probabilistic? ("After I wake up"
    is probabilistic — your wake context varies. "After my feet hit
    the floor" is deterministic.)
  - When the anchor happened, was your brain in a state where it
    could even register the prompt? ("After I sit at my desk" doesn't
    work if you're already on Slack the second you sit down.)
  - Was there ANY moment in your day when you thought "oh right, I
    was supposed to ___" but it was already too late? That's a
    prompt-timing problem, not a willpower problem.

If the answer to any of these is "yes — the anchor wasn't right,"
that's your fix. Re-pick the anchor.

The most common fix: move from a vague anchor (morning routine) to
a deterministic one (specific physical moment that happens daily).
```

Common prompt fixes:

| Bad anchor | Good anchor |
|---|---|
| "When I wake up" | "After my feet touch the floor" |
| "In the morning" | "After I press start on the coffee machine" |
| "When I get to work" | "After I open my laptop and the screen wakes up" |
| "Before bed" | "After I plug my phone in to charge" |
| "When I sit down to work" | "After I sit down AND close email" |

## Diagnosis 2 — ABILITY failure

```
Second check: was the behavior actually small enough?

The brutal test: could you do it on your WORST day? Sick. Sleep-deprived.
After a fight. Period day. Post-flight. Sad. Hungover.

If the answer is "well, on a good day yes" — the behavior is too big.
The book's claim: a behavior that requires a good day to do isn't a
habit, it's a mood-contingent behavior.

Cut by 50% and try again. "10 minutes of meditation" → "3 breaths."
"5 push-ups" → "1 push-up." "Write 1 sentence" → "Open the doc."

Counter-intuitive: cutting the behavior down doesn't make it less
useful — it makes it more likely to actually happen, and once habituated
the larger version emerges on its own (Fogg's "habits grow" principle).
```

The Ability Chain (5 links):

```
Ability isn't just "how hard is the behavior." Five links:

  1. TIME — does it require time you don't have? (cut duration)
  2. MONEY — does it require money you don't have? (cut cost)
  3. PHYSICAL EFFORT — does it require energy you don't have? (cut intensity)
  4. MENTAL EFFORT — does it require thinking you don't have? (cut decisions)
  5. ROUTINE FIT — does it conflict with what you already do? (re-anchor)

The weakest link is the bottleneck. Strengthen that one — usually
by making the behavior tinier.
```

## Diagnosis 3 — CELEBRATION skipped (the silent killer)

```
Third check, often the actual answer: did you celebrate every rep?

Most people who do "tiny habits" by name skip this part — feels silly,
feels optional. It's not optional.

Fogg's load-bearing claim: emotion at completion is what wires the
habit. Behavior without celebration doesn't get encoded as automatic.
You can do the same tiny behavior for 30 days without celebration and
it stays effortful; you can do it for 7 days WITH real celebration
and it starts feeling automatic.

If you've been doing the recipe without the celebration step — that's
probably the missing piece. Add a real 1-second celebration after the
next 3 reps and see if anything shifts.

If celebration felt fake — switch to a different one. There's an
appendix of 100+ techniques because one method doesn't fit everyone.
```

## Diagnosis 4 — only NOW we look at motivation

```
If prompt, ability, and celebration all check out and the habit still
isn't sticking, only then we look at motivation.

But here's where Fogg parts ways from popular advice: the answer is
NOT "find more motivation." The answer is "the behavior wasn't yours."

Common cases:
  - You picked a behavior that serves someone else's image of you
    (parent, partner, social media), not yours
  - The aspiration isn't actually important to you right now (you're
    in survival mode for other reasons)
  - The behavior conflicts with another genuine value (e.g., "meditate
    daily" but you're an extrovert who recharges from people)

If this is the diagnosis, the fix isn't to push through — it's to
re-pick a different behavior that comes from genuine want, not from
"I should."

This sometimes means: stop trying to build this particular habit.
That's a valid answer. The Atomic Habits identity-vote frame ("am
I voting for a person who does X?") is helpful here for checking.
```

## Closer (type C — re-entry + freedom)

```
You picked a letter. We tried a fix.

This week, do the recipe with the fix in place. Don't tell me unless
you want to.

Next week or whenever, come back and say:
  - "sticking" → great, we're done; let it run
  - "still failing" → we go to the next diagnosis (e.g., if we fixed
    prompt and it still fails, we move to ability)
  - "fixed something else by accident" → tell me what shifted

Not coming back is also fine.
```

## Special case: user has tried 5+ habits and they all fail

Sometimes a user has a pattern, not a single failure. Treat differently:

```
You mentioned tried-and-failed multiple times. That's a different
problem than one failed habit.

Possible structural causes:
  - Life situation isn't compatible with habit work right now (new
    baby, acute job stress, recent major loss, chronic illness flare).
    Tiny Habits assumes baseline stability. If you don't have it,
    no method will hold.
  - The aspiration the habits serve isn't yours. Pick someone else's
    goal, no method will make it stick.
  - Undiagnosed condition (ADHD especially — see Atomic Habits skill's
    vulnerable_clinical exit for the routing pattern).

Which feels closest? Or none?

If none: pick the ONE smallest behavior you've ever felt curious about
(not "should") and we redesign with extra attention to celebration.
Sometimes a single felt micro-win unlocks the pattern.
```

---

## What this scene doesn't do

- Doesn't tell the user "you need more discipline" (the book's whole point)
- Doesn't suggest accountability partners, streaks, or apps as the primary fix (those are downstream; if PROMPT and ABILITY and CELEBRATION are wrong, no app helps)
- Doesn't reassure with "everyone struggles with this" (true but useless)
