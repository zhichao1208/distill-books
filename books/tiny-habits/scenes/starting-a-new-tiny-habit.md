# Scene: starting a new tiny habit

## Trigger

User says "I want to start X" + a specific desired behavior (flossing, meditating, push-ups, journaling, writing, stretching, drinking water...).

Triggers main SKILL.md cold-start A.

## Three pieces, in order

### 1. Convert aspiration → behavior (Fogg's first move)

Most users name an aspiration ("be healthier," "write more") rather than a behavior. Fogg's Ch. 2: aspirations can't be designed, only behaviors can.

```
Quick check on what you said. Three types of things:

  Aspiration: "be healthier" — a direction, can't be done in a day
  Outcome:    "lose 10 pounds" — a result, can't be done in a day either
  Behavior:   "walk 20 min after lunch" — a thing you actually do

You can't design an aspiration. You can only design behaviors that
serve an aspiration.

If you said an aspiration, swap to a specific behavior. Use Fogg's
Magic Wand question: "If I could wave a wand, what specific behaviors
would I be doing every day to serve this aspiration?"

Brainstorm 5-10 behaviors (Swarm of Bs). Don't filter yet. We'll pick.
```

### 2. Pick the one Golden Behavior using Focus Mapping

Once user has a swarm of candidate behaviors, narrow with a 2×2:

```
For each behavior, rate on a gut 1-10:
  Impact: if you did this consistently, how much would it serve your aspiration?
  Feasibility: how realistic is it that you'd actually do it daily?

Plot:
              IMPACT
        Low  ←——————→  High
   F   ╔═══════════════════════╗
   E   ║  doesn't matter / no  ║
   A   ║  worth designing      ║   ← top-right = GOLDEN
   S H ║                       ║      (high impact + actually doable)
   I i ║          GOLDEN       ║
   B g ║                       ║
   I h ╠═══════════════════════╣
   L   ║                       ║
   I L ║                       ║
   T o ║  high-aspiration but  ║   ← top-left = the trap
   Y w ║  unrealistic           ║      (this is where most people
       ╚═══════════════════════╝       set themselves up to fail)

Tell me your top 3 candidates and we'll pick the Golden one.
```

### 3. Make it TINY (Fogg's load-bearing rule)

Take the chosen behavior and cut it until it can be done on the worst day:

| User says | Cut to |
|---|---|
| "Meditate 10 minutes" | "Take 3 deep breaths" |
| "Write 500 words" | "Write 1 sentence" |
| "20 push-ups" | "1 push-up" or "get on the floor" |
| "Floss daily" | "Floss one tooth" |
| "Read 30 pages" | "Open the book to page 1" |
| "Run 5km" | "Put on running shoes" |

**Two valid tactics**:
- **Starter Step**: the first physical action of the larger behavior (e.g., "put on running shoes" as starter for running)
- **Scaled-Back Version**: a miniature of the whole behavior (e.g., "2 push-ups" as miniature of a workout)

Pick one. Don't pick "do whichever feels right" — that's not a recipe.

### 4. Pick the anchor

Match by frequency / location / theme:

```
Pick an existing daily routine you do without thinking. Your anchor
must match the new behavior on at least one of these:

  Frequency: daily anchor for daily habit. Don't anchor a daily 
             behavior to a weekly event.
  Location:  if the behavior is in the kitchen, the anchor should be
             in the kitchen.
  Theme:     a "morning calm" habit anchors better to a morning
             calm routine than to "after my afternoon meetings."

Reliable anchor candidates:
  - Brushing teeth (morning + night)
  - Pouring first coffee
  - Sitting down at desk
  - Plugging phone in to charge
  - Closing laptop at end of day
  - Locking front door when arriving home
  - Pressing the elevator button

NOT reliable anchors:
  - "When I wake up" (too vague — wake context varies)
  - "When I have time" (not an anchor)
  - "In the evening" (not specific)

Tell me your anchor.
```

### 5. The celebration — most users skip this; Fogg insists

```
Right after the behavior — within 5 seconds — do a 1-second emotion.
This is the wiring step. Skipping it is the most common reason
Fogg's method fails for new readers.

Options (pick what feels real to you):
  Physical: fist pump, raise arms like you scored, snap fingers,
            quick dance step, point at yourself in the mirror
  Verbal:   "Yes!", "Boom!", "I did that!", "Hell yeah", your own
            word that comes naturally
  Internal: imagine a crowd cheering, see yourself smile in mind's
            eye (use only if external feels truly impossible)

It will feel silly the first 5 times. That's the signal you're
doing it right — fake emotion doesn't wire, only real felt emotion
does. The book's 100+ celebration techniques (appendix) exist
because if one stops feeling real, you switch.
```

### Recipe output (closer type A)

```
Recipe:
  After I [anchor], I will [tiny behavior].
  To celebrate, I will [shine].

Tonight, do it. Don't tell me after.

In a week, come back if you want. Say "sticking" / "failing" / 
"changed it." Any of those works. Not coming back is also fine.
```

---

## Failure cases

- **User picks an anchor that's not actually reliable.** Comes back day 5 saying "I keep forgetting." → That's data: the anchor was probabilistic, not deterministic. Re-pick. Common: "after I wake up" doesn't work because wake-up context varies; "after my feet hit the floor" does because it's the same physical moment every morning.

- **User refuses to celebrate ("it's silly").** Don't argue, but explain once: "Fake-it works for some methods, not Fogg's. The celebration is the wiring. If you skip it, you're doing a different method — and Atomic Habits might fit you better." Then either accept (with caveat) or redirect.

- **User picks a Golden Behavior that's actually still too big.** Day 3 fail. Cut by 50% and re-pick. Repeat until done-on-worst-day is true.

- **User comes back saying "I did it for 7 days but now I'm getting bored."** Good — Fogg's "habits grow on their own" kicks in here. The behavior wants to expand naturally. Don't force expansion; let user discover it. If they want to scale up, design a new tiny ABC for the bigger version.

---

## What this scene doesn't do

- It does NOT prescribe a specific behavior (must be the user's pick, even if "wrong")
- It does NOT recommend any specific number of days to try (Fogg explicitly rejects "21 days" / "66 days")
- It does NOT use the word "willpower" approvingly
