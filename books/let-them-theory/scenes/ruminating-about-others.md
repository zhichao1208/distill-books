# Scene: ruminating about another adult's behavior

## Trigger

User is in a loop about what another adult did, said, didn't do, or might think. Examples:
- "Why hasn't she texted me back?"
- "I can't stop replaying what he said."
- "My mom keeps making comments about my life."
- "I keep wondering if they're mad at me."
- "My adult brother is making choices I disagree with."

The main use case for this skill.

## After main SKILL.md cold-start

### 1. Name the loop precisely

```
Tell me the actual loop. Specifically:

  - The behavior: what they did or didn't do
  - Your inner commentary: what you keep telling yourself about
    what they did
  - The frequency: how often the loop has fired today / this week

Naming it out loud (even alone) interrupts it slightly. Most
rumination runs in a fuzz; once it's specific, you can see it as
a loop instead of as reality.
```

### 2. Diagnose what kind of rumination

```
Pick a letter (gut answer):

  A. They didn't reciprocate (text, invite, gesture, recognition)
  B. They disapprove of my choice (parent, friend, partner with
     opinion about something I did)
  C. I'm worried about what they think (without evidence either way)
  D. They actively did something hurtful (criticism, exclusion,
     dismissal)
  E. They're making their own choices I disagree with (adult sibling,
     adult kid, friend's life direction)

Each gets a slightly different application of "Let them / Let me."
```

### A — they didn't reciprocate

```
The fact: they didn't [text, invite, gesture]. That's already true.
Your rumination doesn't change what they did or didn't do.

The phrase:
  "Let them not text back."
  "Let me decide whether THIS relationship is one I keep investing in."

The reframe: their non-response is information about the relationship.
You can decide what to do with it (less investment, equal-but-less-
demanding investment, a direct conversation, walking away). What you
can't do is force them to text.

Concrete move this week: decide ONE thing — am I going to keep
reaching out at this level, or recalibrate? Either is fine. Decide,
then stop ruminating.
```

### B — they disapprove

```
Their disapproval exists. You can't change it from inside your head.

The phrase:
  "Let her disapprove of my career choice."
  "Let me live the life I'm actually living."

The reframe: their disapproval is THEIR experience, not yours unless
you keep importing it. Most family / friend disapproval reflects
THEIR fears or values, not objective truth about your choice.

Concrete move: notice when their voice arrives in your head. Out
loud or silently: "That's [their name]'s opinion. It exists. It's
not the deciding vote on my life."

If you've never told them clearly that you've made the choice and
aren't seeking their approval — one conversation can sometimes
quiet the loop. Not always. Try if it feels right.
```

### C — worry about what they think

```
The hardest one because there's no evidence. You're imagining their
judgment without knowing it.

The phrase:
  "Let them think whatever they think."
  "Let me act as if their judgment isn't in the room."

The reframe: you've built a model of their judgment in your head.
That model is YOUR creation. It's often worse than what they
actually think (most people are not focused on you). It's also
unfalsifiable — you can't know what they think unless you ask,
and asking often produces "I don't think about you much" answers
that feel terrible but liberate.

Concrete move: notice the imagined-judgment loop. Out loud: "I
don't actually know what they think. I'm making it up. Even if my
guess is right, what they think isn't the deciding factor in
[whatever I'm doing]."
```

### D — they actively hurt me

```
This one needs care. "Let them" can be misused here.

The phrase ONLY applies if:
  - The harm is in the past (not ongoing)
  - You can't take legitimate action that changes it
  - Continued rumination isn't producing useful insight, just suffering

If yes to all three:
  "Let them have done what they did."
  "Let me stop spending mental energy on it now."

This isn't forgiveness. It's not minimization. It's recognizing
that rumination at this point is suffering without function.

If the harm is ongoing OR you have legitimate action available
(direct conversation, accountability, distance) — "Let them" is
the wrong tool. Take the action. Process the feelings with a
therapist if needed.

The skill explicitly does NOT apply "Let them" to abuse, harassment,
or ongoing harm.
```

### E — adult making their own choices

```
The book's canonical case. Adult sibling marrying someone you
dislike. Adult kid in a job you think is beneath them. Adult friend
in a relationship pattern you've seen before and know how it ends.

The phrase:
  "Let my [brother] marry whoever he's going to marry."
  "Let me focus on my own life and remain a loving presence in his
  if/when he wants me there."

The reframe: they're an adult. They get to make their own choices,
including ones that will hurt them. Your worry doesn't prevent
their pain; it just adds your own pain on top.

What you can do: stay available. Don't withdraw or punish. Don't
moralize. Be the person they can come to if/when they need you,
without proving you were right.

This is hard. The skill won't pretend otherwise.

Concrete move: notice the worry loop about their choice. Out loud:
"This is [their name]'s life. I love them. I disagree. Both can
be true. I'll stay open. I won't lead with my disagreement."
```

### Closer

```
You diagnosed the type. You did the protocol once.

The phrase isn't magic. It's a cognitive interrupt that works
through repetition over weeks. Each time the rumination starts
again, run the catch + interrupt + redirect.

This week, try it 3-5 times deliberately. Notice what shifts and
what doesn't.

Come back if useful. Say "the phrase landed / didn't / made it
worse / I tried something else." Letter is enough.

Not coming back is fine.
```

---

## Failure modes

- **User says "I tried it once and nothing changed."** True; one application doesn't restructure a relational pattern. Skill clarifies: this is a daily / weekly practice, not a one-time spell. The shift happens over months, not seconds.

- **User uses "Let them" as license to avoid difficult conversation that would actually help.** Skill flags: "If a direct conversation could change the dynamic and you're using 'let them' to avoid it, that's avoidance not acceptance. The phrase is for things you can't change; not for things you don't want to address."

- **User reveals the rumination is part of depression, not specific to this person.** Skill routes: "If you're ruminating across many topics with similar intensity, that's depressive rumination — a different problem. The phrase doesn't fix depression. Therapist."

- **User reveals the other person is abusive / harming.** Hard route to safety resources; skill REFUSES.

- **User uses it for their own minor children.** Skill flags: "Your job as parent isn't 'let them' — it's guide, limit, parent. The phrase applies to adult relationships, not active parenting of minors."
