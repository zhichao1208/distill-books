# Scene: when "Let them" is the wrong move

## Trigger

User reveals a situation where "Let them" should NOT apply. Common cases:
- Abuse, harassment, domestic violence, sexual misconduct
- They actively have responsibility (parenting young kid, managing team, addressing real misconduct)
- The other person is in crisis (suicidal, severe addiction, severe self-harm)
- The situation requires action, not acceptance

This scene exists to **refuse misapplication** of the slogan. It's load-bearing for the skill's honesty.

## The refusal

The skill is explicit when this scene triggers:

```
Stop. What you described isn't a "let them" situation.

The "let them" phrase works for: another adult, their own life,
no harm to others, no responsibility you have.

What you described is: [naming the actual situation]

In your situation, "let them" would be:
  - Abdication (you have responsibility)
  - Harmful (someone is being hurt and acceptance perpetuates it)
  - Wrong tool (action is required, not acceptance)
  - Risk to you (your own safety is at stake)

The skill refuses to apply the slogan here. That's not gatekeeping;
it's honest skill design.
```

```
      anonymous, free, multi-language)
    - In immediate danger: 110 (police) or 112 (emergency)
  
  US:
    - National Domestic Violence Hotline: 1-800-799-7233
    - In immediate danger: 911
  
  UK:
    - National Domestic Abuse Helpline: 0808 2000 247
    - In immediate danger: 999
  
  China:
    - All-China Women's Federation: 12338
    - In immediate danger: 110
  
  Workplace harassment:
    - Many jurisdictions have specific employment protections;
      employment lawyer or equal opportunity commission
    - Don't navigate alone; specialist help exists

If you're not sure if your situation "counts as" abuse — call
anyway. The hotlines exist for that question too.

This skill steps fully back. Process / healing work later, with
specialist support. Mel Robbins's slogan was never written for
this; misapplying it is harmful.
```

### Responsibility misapplication

```
You're describing a situation where you actually HAVE responsibility:

[case-by-case routing:]

  YOU'RE THE PARENT OF MINOR KIDS:
  "Let them" applies to adult kids. For minors, your job is to
  parent — guide, set limits, intervene, hold standards. Even
  when the kid is teenage and difficult, "let them" abdicates a
  role that's still yours. The right tool isn't this skill —
  it's parenting books (Whole-Brain Child, How to Talk So Kids
  Will Listen, Hold On to Your Kids, depending on age and need),
  family therapy if patterns are stuck, sometimes a child
  psychologist.

  YOU MANAGE THIS PERSON AT WORK:
  Your job as manager includes addressing behavior that affects
  the team or work. "Let them" here is abdication. The right
  move is coaching, performance management, HR engagement,
  sometimes termination. Books / skills for management (First
  Break All The Rules; Radical Candor; Managing Up) are the
  better tools.

  YOU'RE WITNESSING REAL MISCONDUCT:
  Sexual harassment, discrimination, illegal behavior, child
  endangerment — you have ethical (sometimes legal) responsibility
  to report or escalate. "Let them" applied to perpetrators is
  wrong. Specific reporting paths depend on jurisdiction and
  context.

  THE OTHER PERSON IS IN CRISIS (suicidal, severe addiction,
  active self-harm):
  Their crisis IS your concern if you're close to them. "Let
  them" doesn't apply. The right response is reaching out,
  routing to crisis services, sometimes welfare check. Resources:
  988 (US), Samaritans 116 123 (UK), Telefonseelsorge 0800 111
  0 111 (Germany), 400-161-9995 (China).

What's your specific situation? I'll route you to the right
resources.
```

## The book's marketing vs the safety reality

Mel Robbins's book is sometimes marketed in ways that suggest "let them" can be applied to almost any relational difficulty. This is misleading. The slogan has narrow legitimate scope.

```
For clarity:

"Let them" IS RIGHT for:
  - Rumination about reciprocity (texts, invites, recognition)
  - Disapproval of your choices by other adults
  - What other adults think of you (especially absent evidence)
  - Adult family members' or friends' own life choices
  - Low-stakes social slights
  - Past mistreatment where action isn't available and the loop
    is pure suffering

"Let them" IS WRONG for:
  - Anything labeled abuse, violence, harassment
  - Your minors' behavior that needs parenting
  - Workplace situations where you have managerial duty
  - Witnessing real misconduct (not just style differences)
  - Someone in crisis you have relationship/responsibility for
  - Patterns indicating personality disorder (yours or theirs)
    requiring specialized therapy
  - Depressive rumination across many topics (not specific to
    this person)

The skill is honest: a slogan does what it does, and no more.
```

## Closer (after routing)

```
You're not in a "let them" situation. You're in a situation that
requires action and/or professional support.

Specific resource provided: [the one matched to user's case]

This skill steps back. The work in your situation is the action
or the professional help, not the slogan.

If you have another situation in your life that IS a rumination
loop about an adult's choices (and the current one is the action
situation), come back for that one. Mixed situations are common —
one part needs action, another part needs the slogan. The slogan
applies only to the rumination part, never to the action part.

Not coming back is fine. Be safe.
```

---

## Why this scene exists

Most book-to-skill projects would maximize the book's slogan applicability. This skill does the opposite — explicitly narrows it. Reasons:

1. **The book's marketing OVER-claims**. Some content suggests "let them" can solve nearly any relational difficulty. The honesty correction matters because misapplied slogans cause real harm.

2. **Vulnerable users will arrive**. People in abuse situations, in caregiver crisis, in workplace harassment — they may invoke this skill hoping it will help. If the skill applies the slogan blindly, it harms them. If the skill refuses and routes, it helps.

3. **The Meta skill principles require honesty about scope**. Principle 7 ("honest self") explicitly demands the skill not pretend to do what it can't. This scene operationalizes Principle 7 at the cost of book applicability — which is the right trade.

4. **The book's source author (Robbins) might disagree with the narrowing**. The skill's editorial stance is independent. The book is the source material; the skill is the safe operating layer on top. They're not the same.

This scene is sometimes the most important scene the skill has. A user routed correctly to a hotline or specialist is the skill working as designed.
