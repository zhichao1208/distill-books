# Scene: when tactics aren't working

## Trigger

User has tried sleep-hygiene tactics for 2-4+ weeks and reports no improvement: "I've tried everything" / "I've done all the standard advice and I still can't sleep" / "Sleep hygiene isn't working for me."

This scene exists to **route to professional care, not give more tactics**.

## First: validate that the tactics had a fair trial

```
Quick check before we go to professional referral — was the trial fair?

  - Caffeine cutoff at 14:00 for 7+ consecutive days?
  - Same wake time every day (within 30 min) for 2+ weeks?
  - Alcohol-free for at least 7 consecutive nights?
  - Bedroom cool (<20°C), dark, quiet?
  - Phone out of bedroom?

If you can answer yes to all 5 for at least 2 weeks and sleep
didn't improve — sleep hygiene isn't the bottleneck for you.
Time for a different tool.

If you can answer yes to less than 4: the trial wasn't complete.
The 1-2 you skipped may be the actual lever. Tighten and try
another 2 weeks before escalating.
```

## If sleep hygiene was tried fairly and didn't work — three escalation paths

### Path A: Insomnia disorder → CBT-I

```
If your pattern is:
  - Difficulty falling asleep AND/OR staying asleep
  - 3+ nights per week
  - For 3+ months
  - Causing daytime distress or dysfunction

That's insomnia disorder (DSM-5 definition). The strongest-evidence
intervention is CBT-I — not sleep hygiene, not pills.

CBT-I outperforms sleep medications in meta-analyses, and the
effects last after treatment stops. Sleeping pills don't.

Options:
  - CBT-I therapist (search "CBT-I" + your area; sleep medicine
    centers often have one)
  - Apps with real evidence:
    - Sleepio (UK NHS-prescribed, paid)
    - Somryst (FDA-cleared)
    - CBT-I Coach (US Veterans Affairs, free)
  - Some sleep clinics offer 4-6 session brief CBT-I

CBT-I includes stimulus control (bed = sleep only), sleep restriction
(temporarily reduce time in bed to consolidate sleep, then expand),
cognitive restructuring around sleep anxiety. It's structured and
short (4-8 weeks typically).

This is the most evidence-based move you can make if sleep hygiene
didn't work.
```

### Path B: Suspected medical cause → sleep study

```
If your pattern includes ANY of these:
  - Snoring (especially loud, witnessed pauses, gasping awake)
  - Daytime sleepiness despite "enough" sleep
  - Morning headaches
  - Dry mouth in morning
  - Restless legs / urge to move legs at night
  - High blood pressure that's hard to control
  - Acid reflux at night
  - Sweating at night (in a cool room)

That's suspicion of a medical sleep disorder (apnea, restless legs,
GERD, hormonal). Tactics won't fix these.

Next step: primary care doctor, ask for sleep study referral. Home
sleep studies are now common, non-invasive, often insurance-covered.

In Germany: Hausarzt → Schlafmedizin / Lungenarzt
In US: PCP → pulmonologist or sleep medicine
In UK: GP → NHS sleep clinic
In China: 综合医院的呼吸科 / 睡眠中心

Don't keep trying sleep hygiene if you have apnea signals — you'll
sleep "right" and still be exhausted, because the issue is breathing
not behavior.
```

### Path C: Mental health is the load-bearing issue

```
If your sleep started or worsened around:
  - A grief event (death, breakup, major loss)
  - A trauma (accident, assault, severe stressor)
  - Onset of depression or anxiety symptoms
  - Major life transition (move, job loss, divorce)
  - Cycle change (perimenopause, postpartum, medication change)

Sleep is downstream of these. Tactics applied on top of an unprocessed
underlying issue plateau quickly.

Next steps:
  - For grief / trauma: a therapist who treats those specifically
    (trauma-focused CBT, EMDR, complicated grief therapy)
  - For depression / anxiety: a psychologist or psychiatrist; sleep
    often improves once underlying mood issue is treated
  - For hormonal: gynecologist, endocrinologist, primary care
  - For medication side effects: physician who prescribed the
    medication; some drugs notoriously disrupt sleep (some
    antidepressants, beta-blockers, steroids, ADHD meds)

Sleep tactics still help marginally — but the larger work is the
underlying issue. This skill steps back here.
```

## What this scene refuses to do

- Give "one more tactic to try" when the user has already tried 2+ weeks of fair sleep hygiene
- Recommend specific sleeping pills, supplements, or melatonin doses
- Suggest sleep trackers as a substitute for medical evaluation
- Tell the user to "just try harder" — the whole point of this scene is acknowledging when tactics stop being the answer

## Closer

```
Three paths above. Pick the one that fits your situation:

  A. Chronic insomnia pattern → CBT-I (therapist or app)
  B. Medical signals (snoring, restless legs, daytime drowsy despite
     hours) → sleep study via primary care
  C. Underlying mental health / life event → therapist for the
     underlying issue, sleep improves downstream

Book the appointment this week (or the app).

This skill is here later for behavioral work after the medical /
clinical path is in motion. Sleep hygiene becomes useful AGAIN once
the underlying issue is being addressed.

Don't come back for more tactics until you've taken the professional
step. The tactics aren't more right just because you've tried them
twice as hard.
```

---

## Failure modes

- **User insists on more tactics, refuses professional referral.** Skill holds the line: "I'd be doing you a disservice to give you more tactics that haven't worked. The honest answer is that this skill has reached the limit of what it can usefully add for your situation."

- **User says they don't have access to professional care (cost, geography, etc.).** Skill acknowledges this is real: "If a clinician isn't accessible, the evidence-based CBT-I apps (Sleepio, Somryst, CBT-I Coach for US veterans) are the closest substitute. They're structured and have stronger evidence than continued sleep hygiene tweaks."

- **User reveals chronic mental health context only at this point.** Skill thanks them for telling and routes: "That's important context that should have come first. The sleep issue and the mental health issue are not separate — they're feedback-looping. The therapist work is the higher-leverage move; sleep tactics resume usefulness after the underlying work starts."
