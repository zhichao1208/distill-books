# Scene: caffeine or alcohol disrupting sleep

## Trigger

User explicitly identifies a substance ("I think coffee is wrecking my sleep" / "I sleep terribly after drinking" / "I cut caffeine but it didn't help"). Or skill detects substance signal from cold-start A diagnosis.

## Caffeine path

### What the data supports

```
Caffeine science (the parts that hold up):

  - Half-life 5-7 hours typical adult. Some people (slow metabolizers,
    CYP1A2 gene) take 8-10 hours.
  - Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors. Adenosine builds during the
    day and creates sleep pressure. Blocking it = "still feel awake"
    even when you're sleep-deprived.
  - When caffeine clears, the accumulated adenosine hits at once —
    the "crash."
  - Tolerance develops to some effects (alertness) but NOT to sleep
    disruption. Daily coffee drinker still has the half-life issue.

What this means for cutoff:
  - 8 hours before sleep is a defensible rule for most people
  - Slow metabolizers: 10-12 hours (no coffee after lunch)
  - If you fall asleep fine on later caffeine but wake at 3am — you
    may be falling through sleep stages too lightly because of the
    caffeine, masking as "I slept but didn't"
```

### Concrete next step

```
For 7 nights, do this:

  - Caffeine cutoff at 14:00 (2pm). All coffee, all black tea,
    green tea, most cola, energy drinks, dark chocolate after lunch.
  - Track ONE thing: how long it takes you to fall asleep, and
    whether you wake during the night.

If 7 nights of caffeine cutoff brings noticeable change → keep it.
If no change → caffeine wasn't the bottleneck; look at alcohol,
screens, or schedule.

If you "need" caffeine after 14:00 to function — that's the signal
of sleep debt, not the answer. The skill won't help bypass it; the
sleep debt is itself the thing to address.

Decaffeinated coffee, herbal tea, kombucha (often has caffeine,
check), matcha, dark chocolate — all sources to track if you're
cutting precisely.
```

## Alcohol path

### What the data supports

```
Alcohol and sleep (the parts that hold up):

  - Alcohol is a CNS depressant — gets you to sleep faster.
  - Once metabolized (3-4 hours after last drink), it switches:
    fragments REM, increases arousal in second half of night,
    often causes 3-5am wake.
  - Even one drink measurably affects sleep architecture in
    sleep studies.
  - The "I sleep better after wine" perception is the sedation
    masking the architecture damage. You feel like you slept; you
    didn't sleep well.
  - REM in particular is wrecked — REM is when memory consolidation
    and emotional regulation happen, so chronic alcohol disrupts
    these even without overt insomnia.

The cleanest demonstration: track sleep quality across nights with
and without alcohol. Most people see a difference within 5 alcohol-
free nights.
```

### Concrete next step

```
Two paths depending on your honesty with yourself:

  A. If you drink most nights and assume sleep is "fine":
     Take 7 consecutive alcohol-free nights. Notice what changes
     in morning energy, recovery from exercise, mood stability,
     and whether 3-5am wakes change frequency.
     This isn't moral about drinking. It's diagnostic.

  B. If you already know alcohol affects sleep and you're not
     able to stop:
     The skill stops at this point. If alcohol use can't be reduced
     despite knowing it costs sleep, that pattern is worth talking
     to a clinician about (not because every night = problem drinking,
     but because lack of ability to change a behavior despite
     knowing the cost is the diagnostic question worth bringing
     to a professional).

If you do the 7-night break and see a difference — the data is
yours. Decide what to do with it.
```

## When both are at play

Most readers have both caffeine after 14:00 AND alcohol most evenings. The combination produces:
- Faster onset (alcohol sedation)
- Lighter second half (alcohol fragmentation)
- Lighter first half too (caffeine still in system)
- Net: feels like sleep, isn't

```
Run them in sequence, not parallel:
  - Week 1: caffeine cutoff at 14:00. Notice baseline change.
  - Week 2: add alcohol cutoff (or 5 nights/week alcohol-free).
  - Week 3: assess what's improved and which factor mattered most.

Trying to change both at once gives you no diagnostic. Sequential
isolation tells you which substance was the main driver.
```

## Closer

```
Pick one this week:
  A. Caffeine cutoff at 14:00 for 7 days
  B. 7 alcohol-free nights
  C. Both (only if you're confident you can stick to both — most
     people fail at both and learn nothing)

Track ONE thing: how long to fall asleep + whether you wake at night.

Come back in a week. Say "different / same." Letter is enough.
Not coming back is fine.
```

---

## Failure modes

- **User does the cutoff for 2 days, says "didn't help."** Two days isn't enough; sleep architecture takes 5-7 days to re-stabilize after removing a daily substance.
- **User has been cutting caffeine for weeks, no change.** Then caffeine wasn't the bottleneck — likely alcohol, schedule variance, or something else. Move on.
- **User can't reduce alcohol despite knowing the cost.** Route to professional (not because of moral judgment, but because the inability-to-change-despite-knowing pattern is diagnostic).
- **User has chronic 3-5am wakes with no alcohol and no caffeine.** Rule out sleep apnea (snoring + tired + maybe partner-observed breathing pauses). If no apnea suspicion, route to CBT-I for the chronic pattern.
