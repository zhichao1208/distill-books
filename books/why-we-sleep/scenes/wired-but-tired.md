# Scene: wired but tired

## Trigger

User is in present tense: "I can't fall asleep" / "I'm exhausted but can't sleep" / "It's 1am and I'm still wide awake."

Most common acute sleep complaint.

## After main SKILL.md cold-start A

### 1. Diagnose by letter (4 most likely causes)

Already presented in cold-start A. Letter routes:

**A. Caffeine after 14:00 today**
```
That's likely the issue tonight specifically.

Caffeine half-life is 5-7 hours. Coffee at 15:00 = ~50% still in
you at 22:00. Some people metabolize even slower (genetic).

Tonight options (limited because the caffeine is already in you):
  - Don't try harder to sleep. Trying creates arousal, worsens the
    problem. Read something boring in dim light. Sleep will arrive
    when the caffeine drops enough.
  - Wake at your normal time tomorrow even if late tonight — keeps
    schedule intact, prevents domino effect

Tomorrow forward:
  - Caffeine cutoff at 14:00 (or earlier if you're a slow metabolizer)
  - If you "need" caffeine after 14:00 to function, that's a sleep
    debt signal — the answer is more sleep not more caffeine

Stop fighting tonight's sleep. Let tomorrow's caffeine math fix it.
```

**B. Alcohol within 3 hours**
```
Two things alcohol does:
  - Initial sedation: feels like it helps you fall asleep
  - Second-half-of-night: metabolizes, fragments REM, often wakes
    you 3-5am

Even small amounts (1 drink) measurably affect sleep architecture.

Tonight: nothing fixes this for the half that's already metabolized.
You'll likely fall asleep faster than expected, then have a fragmented
second half.

Going forward:
  - 3-hour minimum gap between last drink and sleep
  - Better: alcohol-free 5 days/week, see if sleep changes
  - If you can't reduce alcohol despite knowing it hurts sleep,
    that's worth looking at separately
```

**C. Screen time before bed**
```
Screen-as-cause is complicated. The book's strong "blue light"
framing is partially overstated; the real issue is usually:
  - Content arousal (work email / scrolling / news) > light wavelength
  - Late-evening cognitive activation makes your brain "still on duty"
  - Phone in hand = social / stimulating, very different from a
    paper book

Tonight: too late for tonight's fix. Tomorrow:
  - 60-min no-work-email window before intended sleep
  - Phone moves to another room at lights-out (not just face down
    on nightstand)
  - Acceptable: paper book, gentle music, conversation, sitting

What you do in the 60 min before bed shapes more than the blue-light-
filter app does.
```

**D. Mind racing about something specific**
```
This is the hardest one tonight because the thought is doing exactly
what your brain wants it to: rehearse and solve.

Tonight only:
  - Get out of bed. Stay in bed = bed becomes associated with anxious
    wakefulness (this is a CBT-I principle that's well-established).
  - Go to another room with dim light. Write down the specific
    thought (or things on your mind list) on paper. The act of
    externalizing often releases the rehearsal loop.
  - When sleepy, return to bed. If wakefulness returns, repeat.

Tomorrow:
  - If this is recurring (not just tonight's specific worry), look at
    whether bedtime is when you finally have no other task — your brain
    may be using bedtime as the only "thinking time" available
  - 5-10 min "worry journal" 1-2 hours before bed (not in bed) can
    pre-process the thoughts
  - For chronic anxiety mind, CBT-I + sometimes CBT for anxiety is
    the durable answer
```

### 2. The meta-rule for tonight

Always state once:

```
Universal rule for tonight: stop fighting it. The brain interprets
"trying hard to sleep" as a threat (something must be wrong if you're
this focused on it), which produces arousal, which prevents sleep.

If you've been trying for 20+ minutes, get out of bed. Dim room.
Boring activity. Return when sleepy. This is CBT-I's stimulus-control
move and it has the strongest evidence in sleep medicine.

Don't watch the clock. Watching the clock creates math ("only 5h
left, only 4h left") that triggers the same arousal cycle.
```

### 3. Tomorrow forward — the small set that holds up

After tonight's situational fix:

```
Three durable changes that work for most people:

  1. Wake at the same time daily, even weekends. Within 30 min.
     This is the single highest-leverage move.
  2. Caffeine cutoff 8-10 hours before intended sleep
  3. Alcohol-free at least 3 nights/week (more if you want to see
     the cleanest sleep architecture)

That's most of the operational core. Skip the wellness-industrial
complex (specific supplements, hyped devices, exotic protocols) until
these three are stable.

Tomorrow morning: same wake time. Notice this evening how you feel.
```

### Closer

```
Tonight: stop fighting. Bed → if not asleep in 20 min → out of bed,
dim activity, return when sleepy.

Tomorrow: same wake time. Caffeine cutoff [time]. Alcohol-free if
possible.

Come back in a week if you want. Say "better / same / worse" — that's
enough. Not coming back is fine.
```

## Out of scope

- Specific sleeping pill recommendations (clinician territory)
- Specific supplement protocols (melatonin doses, magnesium, glycine — clinician territory)
- Sleep tracker interpretation (the consumer trackers' "sleep stages" estimates are poorly validated — don't treat them as truth)
- Polyphasic sleep schedules (evidence weak; skill doesn't recommend)

## Failure cases

- **User says "I tried the 20-min get-out-of-bed thing and it didn't work."**
  → That's not enough data for one night. The technique works over weeks of consistent application, not one attempt. Skill should clarify: "It's not a one-night cure — it's a relationship reset between your brain and the bed. Takes 2-4 weeks of consistent application for most people."

- **User says "but I've been like this for years."**
  → Route to chronic_insomnia exit. This scene is for acute (1-3 weeks), not chronic.

- **User reveals they're going through grief / trauma / major life change.**
  → Acknowledge: sleep disruption is part of grief / major stress; the tactics still help marginally but the larger work is processing the event with support. Skill doesn't pretend tactics fix it. Routes to "if this lasts more than 4-6 weeks, see a therapist."
