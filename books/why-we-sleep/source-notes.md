# Why We Sleep · sources and chapter mapping

## Book

- **Original**: *Why We Sleep: Unlocking the Power of Sleep and Dreams*
- **Author**: Matthew Walker, PhD
- **Publisher**: Scribner / Simon & Schuster, 2017
- **ISBN**: 978-1501144318
- **Chinese edition**: 《我们为什么要睡觉》, 北京联合出版公司, 2018
- **Author background**: UC Berkeley professor of neuroscience and psychology; director of the Center for Human Sleep Science; previously at Harvard Medical School
- **Goodreads**: ~4.31 / 5 across ~140,000+ ratings
- **Cultural footprint**: Joe Rogan appearance, TED talks, Tim Ferriss interviews; "8 hours non-negotiable" became cultural shorthand from this book

## What this skill uses

The skill uses Walker's **operational core** — sleep hygiene tactics, the two-process model (sleep pressure + circadian), caffeine pharmacology, alcohol-REM disruption, the case for CBT-I over pills. These largely hold up across the broader sleep medicine literature.

The skill **does not** repeat Walker's strong disease-causation claims because of well-documented credibility issues (see Critique below).

## CRITICAL: The Guzey 2019 critique and downstream

This is **load-bearing** for the skill's honest stance.

**Alexey Guzey, December 2019**: "Matthew Walker's Why We Sleep Is Riddled with Scientific and Factual Errors" (guzey.com/books/why-we-sleep/). The critique documents:

1. **WHO "sleep loss epidemic" claim is false**. Walker attributes the phrase to WHO; no WHO source uses it. Walker has acknowledged this.

2. **"<6-7 hours sleep doubles cancer risk"** — not supported by larger meta-analyses. The cited studies don't show what Walker claims at the magnitudes claimed.

3. **Linear "shorter sleep = shorter life"** misrepresents the actual U-shaped curve. Risk rises both at very short AND very long sleep; ~7 hours is a floor for most adults, not a linear "more = better."

4. **Fatal Familial Insomnia (FFI) as proof sleep loss kills** — misuse of a rare prion disease to make general population claims about sleep deprivation.

5. **A graph from a key cited paper was apparently manipulated/cropped** in a way that made the original finding appear stronger than it was.

**Andrew Gelman (statistics blog) follow-ups** (2019-2020):
- Initial coverage of Guzey's critique
- "A Tale of Institutional Failure" (March 2020) — about how the book passed peer review-equivalent scrutiny in publishing
- "Statistics as Squid Ink" (CHANCE 2020) — published academic treatment

**Walker's response**: issued a pre-print correction addressing some points; some uncorrected as of 2024; continues to repeat some flagged claims in podcast appearances.

## What the skill DOES carry from Walker

Tactics that hold up across the broader literature (not just Walker):

- Consistent sleep/wake time (within 30 min, even weekends) — strong evidence
- Cool dark quiet room (~18°C / 65°F) — strong
- Caffeine half-life 5-7 hours, cutoff 8-10 hours before sleep — physiologically grounded
- Alcohol disrupts second-half-of-night REM even at small doses — strong evidence
- Morning light exposure sets circadian rhythm — strong
- 7-9 hour adult baseline (with individual variation) — population-level supported
- CBT-I beats sleeping medications long-term — strongest meta-analysis evidence in sleep medicine
- Bed = sleep only (stimulus control) — CBT-I principle, evidence-backed

## What the skill DOES NOT carry

- "WHO sleep loss epidemic" framing — false
- Strong cancer / Alzheimer's mortality claims at Walker's magnitudes — unsupported
- "8 hours non-negotiable" as categorical rule — population baseline, not individual prescription
- Specific causal chains the book asserts beyond evidence support
- Linear "shorter = worse" framing — U-shape is the actual relationship
- Polyphasic sleep recommendations — evidence is weak either way
- Blue light as the primary screen-before-bed issue — content arousal matters more

## Chinese edition notes

The Chinese edition follows the English's structure and includes the strong claims that the English-language critical literature has subsequently challenged. Chinese readers should be aware the Guzey critique has not been as widely translated; the Chinese discourse on Walker's book still mostly accepts the strong framing.

Key terminology:
- Sleep pressure / Process S → 睡眠压力 / S 过程
- Circadian rhythm / Process C → 昼夜节律 / C 过程
- REM/NREM → REM (快速眼动) / NREM (非快速眼动)
- CBT-I → 失眠的认知行为疗法 / CBT-I (often kept in English in academic contexts)
- Stimulus control → 刺激控制
- Sleep restriction → 睡眠限制

## Related / complementary books

- **The Sleep Solution** (W. Chris Winter, 2017) — more clinical, less famous; less overclaim. Good companion read.
- **Outlive** (Attia, 2023) — covers sleep briefly within longevity frame. Different scope.
- **Why We Can't Sleep** (Ada Calhoun) — about midlife sleep issues for women; different demographic angle.
- **The Promise of Sleep** (William Dement, 1999) — earlier sleep classic; less overclaim, less famous.
- **End the Insomnia Struggle** (Ehrnstrom & Brosse) — CBT-I self-help with strong evidence base.

## Important limitations / counterpoints (skill carries these)

- **Walker's strong public-health claims have been challenged.** Skill omits them.
- **Sleep tracker data ("you got 23% deep sleep") is poorly validated.** Skill doesn't treat tracker readouts as truth.
- **Individual variation is real.** Some people are true short sleepers (genetic, very rare). The skill mentions but doesn't certify users as short sleepers — that's a sleep clinic's call.
- **CBT-I is the strongest single intervention** for chronic insomnia. Skill routes there for chronic cases rather than continuing to give tactics.
- **Sleep apnea is medical**, not behavioral. Skill routes promptly.
