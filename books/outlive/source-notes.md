## Chapter → skill field mapping

The book is structured in 3 parts. Part 1 is conceptual (Medicine 3.0 framework), Part 2 covers the 4 horsemen in clinical detail, Part 3 is the lifestyle "tactics."

### Part 1: The framework (heavily used by the skill)

| Chapter | How the skill uses it |
|---|---|
| Ch 1: The Long Game | Background only; the skill assumes the user believes long-horizon thinking matters |
| Ch 2: Medicine 3.0 | **Core skill lens.** Reactive vs proactive medicine framing. Used in cold-start B + C |
| Ch 3: Objective, Strategy, Tactics | Centenarian Decathlon concept introduced here. **Core lens for cold-start A** |
| Ch 4: Centenarians | Background; informs the "what does it take to make 90" framing |

### Part 2: The 4 Horsemen (used as routing taxonomy)

| Chapter / Horseman | How the skill uses it |
|---|---|
| Ch 5: Atherosclerotic Cardiovascular Disease | The ASCVD horseman in the routing table. Skill points to the conversation, doesn't prescribe specific lipid tests. |
| Ch 6: Cancer | The Cancer horseman. Skill discusses screening cadence concept, doesn't recommend specific tests. |
| Ch 7: Alzheimer's & Neurodegenerative | The Brain horseman. Skill discusses modifiable risk factors (sleep, exercise, social) without medicalizing. |
| Ch 8: Metabolic Dysfunction | The Metabolic horseman. Skill points to A1C / metabolic markers as worth tracking with clinician. |

### Part 3: Lifestyle tactics (used cautiously, never as prescription)

| Chapter | How the skill uses it |
|---|---|
| Ch 9: Exercise (the framework) | Categories named in scenes/want-to-start-training.md (strength / Zone 2 / VO2max / stability). Never prescribes program. |
| Ch 10-12: Exercise (the specifics) | Strength, Zone 2, VO2max chapters. **Skill cites the categories, never the exact protocols.** Attia's specific recommendations are for a personal physician + trainer, not for AI to repeat. |
| Ch 13: Nutrition | **Largely not used.** Nutrition is the most personalized area in the book; AI giving generic Attia advice risks misapplication. Skill routes nutrition questions to RDs. |
| Ch 14: Sleep | Mentioned in family-history-fear scene (brain horseman modifiable factors). Skill doesn't prescribe sleep hygiene specifics. |
| Ch 15: Emotional Health | Mentioned briefly; the book's weakest chapter, often felt bolted-on. Skill doesn't lean on it. |

## Concepts deliberately not used (and why)

| Book concept | Why not in skill |
|---|---|
| ApoB specific cutoffs | The book mentions Attia's own targets; AI giving these as "what you should aim for" is medical advice. Skill mentions ApoB by category (as one cardiovascular marker), never by target value. |
| Lp(a) testing recommendation | Same reason. Skill mentions it as something to discuss with physician, doesn't recommend the test itself. |
| Rapamycin / metformin discussion | Attia discusses both as longevity-relevant in the book. AI mentioning these as "consider asking your doctor about" is dangerous — they're prescription drugs with side effects, not supplements. Skill omits entirely. |
| Specific exercise prescriptions (e.g. "do 4 Zone 2 sessions at 60 min each per week") | Even though widely cited, these are individual; AI repeating them is prescription. Skill mentions Zone 2 as a category, never as a protocol. |
| Continuous glucose monitor recommendation | Attia uses CGM personally; book discusses. AI recommending CGM use risks both medical advice (overinterpretation by user) and unnecessary medicalization for users with normal metabolism. Skill doesn't suggest. |
| Specific fasting protocols (TRE, prolonged fasting) | Same — protocol-specific, individual variation high, ED risk. Skill omits. |
| Cold plunges, sauna protocols | Discussed in podcast contexts more than book; AI prescribing specific protocols is unnecessary medicalization for what's lifestyle. Skill doesn't recommend specifics. |
| Specific supplement protocols (creatine, omega-3, etc.) | Even widely-safe supplements need physician input on dose / interaction / contraindications. Skill never recommends. |
| Hormone replacement therapy (HRT) discussion | Major topic in Attia's book; AI recommendation is profoundly inappropriate. Skill routes hormone questions to endocrinology / specialists. |

The aggressive "don't recommend" list is what makes Outlive different from AH and PoM. Medical adjacency requires harder boundaries.

## Related / complementary books

- **The Longevity Diet** (Valter Longo, 2018) — Nutrition-specific; Attia disagrees with several Longo claims (specifically the fasting-mimicking diet specifics). If skill ever covers nutrition, this disagreement is worth noting.
- **Lifespan** (David Sinclair, 2019) — More speculative; heavy on rapamycin / NMN / NAD+ supplements. **Skill will likely never cover Sinclair** — too much overlap with the "do specific supplement X" trap.
- **Why We Sleep** (Matthew Walker, 2017) — Single-domain (sleep). Companion skill planned for v0.5. The Outlive skill points to "discuss sleep with a sleep medicine specialist" rather than prescribing; Why We Sleep skill will own the sleep-specific frame.
- **The Body Keeps the Score** (Bessel van der Kolk) — Adjacent (trauma + body). Planned v0.8. The Outlive skill doesn't go into trauma territory; that's vdk's domain.

## Important limitations / counterpoints

- **The book is written for people with healthcare access.** Many of Attia's recommended conversations assume the user has a primary care physician they see regularly. Users without access (uninsured in US, underserved areas, etc.) get reduced value from the skill. Skill doesn't pretend otherwise.

- **Attia's own clinical practice is high-end concierge medicine.** Some readers find the book's tests-and-monitoring suggestions financially unrealistic. The skill's "frame the conversation with your physician" approach is more universal than the book's clinical density.

- **Critiques of Attia worth knowing**:
  - Some critics (incl. epidemiologists) argue the book overstates the actionability of preventive cardiology; the evidence for some interventions is observational not interventional.
  - The book's heavy emphasis on Zone 2 cardio is well-supported but some experts argue the volume Attia recommends is unrealistic for most.
  - The "Medicine 3.0" framing is partly marketing-positioned; some preventive medicine practitioners have been doing this for decades without the label.
  - The book is implicitly written for relatively healthy 40-60-year-old high-agency readers; less applicable to chronic-disease management or to readers in 70s+.

- **The Outlive skill explicitly NEVER**:
  - Suggests a specific drug or supplement
  - Interprets a user's specific lab value
  - Predicts the user's lifespan or risk percentile
  - Prescribes a training protocol with weight / sets / reps / zones
  - Tells the user "you'll be fine" or "you're at high risk"
