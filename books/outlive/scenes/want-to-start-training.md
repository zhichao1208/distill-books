# Scene: want to start training

## Trigger

User says they want to start exercising / get fit / get strong / be more active — but doesn't know where to start, OR has tried before and stopped.

Common phrasings:
- "I want to start working out but don't know where"
- "I haven't trained in years, I'm out of shape"
- "I want to be strong / fit / mobile when I'm older"
- "I tried running / lifting / yoga and it didn't stick"

## After main SKILL.md cold-start A, enter this scene

### 1. Re-anchor on the Centenarian Decathlon

If the user already named a 90-year scene in cold-start A, use it. If not, get it now:

```
Before any training talk: name the scene at 90.

Not "be fit" or "be strong" — those are abstract. Pick one concrete
physical thing you want to be able to do at age 90:

  - Pick up your grandchild (or a 15kg bag of compost) without strain
  - Climb 3 flights of stairs without stopping to breathe
  - Walk 10km on uneven trail
  - Get off the floor without using your hands
  - Carry your own groceries up to a 4th-floor apartment
  - Throw a ball with grandkids
  - Live alone in your own home

Once you have it, that's the target.
```

### 2. Reverse-engineer broad categories (NOT prescriptions)

```
Attia's frame: whatever you want to do at 90, your body needs to be
considerably stronger NOW to leave margin for the loss that comes
with aging.

Broad categories your training would touch (NOT a program — categories
to discuss with a qualified trainer or physical therapist):

  Strength — to preserve muscle and bone over decades
  Aerobic endurance ("Zone 2") — the long, slow base that protects
    metabolic and cardiovascular health
  Anaerobic / VO2max work — short hard efforts, the "ceiling" of
    cardio capacity that predicts longevity
  Stability and mobility — joint health, fall prevention
  Recovery quality — sleep, nutrition, stress (these aren't training
    but they bottleneck everything)

The book argues all 5 matter. Most people skip strength and stability,
which are the two with the strongest aging-resistance evidence.
```

### 3. Skill does NOT prescribe a program

The temptation to give "2 strength days / 3 Zone 2 / 1 VO2max" is real but **forbidden**. Skill output here:

```
What this skill does NOT give you:
  - Specific weights, reps, sets
  - "X workouts per week" prescription
  - Exact heart rate zones for your Zone 2
  - Diet / supplement / fasting protocol

What you do instead:

  1) Find a qualified strength coach or physical therapist for the
     STRENGTH and STABILITY work. If you have any joint history (knees,
     back, shoulders) or are 50+ starting from sedentary, a PT
     consultation FIRST prevents injuries that derail year 1.
     
  2) For AEROBIC base (Zone 2), the cheapest accurate option is a
     consultation with a sports physician for a lactate test or a
     metabolic cart. Or work with a coach who can use heart-rate
     estimation correctly. "180 minus age" is a rough proxy that's
     close but not accurate for many people.

  3) Start with 1-2 sessions a week in any category. Adding sessions
     before the body adapts is the most common reason people stop.
     The atomic habit skill applies here: 1 session done beats 5
     sessions planned.

  4) Track ONE concrete thing for 12 weeks (not 6). Examples:
     - How many push-ups can you do? Re-test in week 12.
     - How far / how fast can you walk 5km? Re-test in week 12.
     - Can you stand from a chair without using your hands? Re-test.
     12 weeks is the minimum window where you can see real change.
```

### 4. Memory borrow — be specific but not prescriptive

If memory contains training-relevant info:

```
[If memory present] I see in your memory you're {age}, {weight} kg at
{height}, with {constraint — e.g. "TMJ history dictates no heavy
overhead pressing without coach supervision"}, training budget
{X hours/week}.

That tells me three things WITHOUT prescribing anything:
  1) Your weekly training budget caps the categories you can hit
     — picking 2 of the 5 categories per phase is more realistic than
     trying all 5
  2) The {constraint} matters; raise it with whoever programs your
     training
  3) Your {age + body composition} starting point shapes the test
     metrics that make sense for week-12 re-testing
```

### 5. Closer (type A — clinician question + concrete step)

```
This week, do these two things:

  1. Pick ONE specific thing from the Centenarian Decathlon list as
     your 12-week test.
  2. If you have any joint history OR are 50+ starting from sedentary,
     book a 1-hour consult with a physical therapist this month.
     The consult is the foundation; everything else builds on it.

The training itself can wait until you've done step 2. Skipping the
PT consult is the most common path to a knee / back injury that costs
6-12 months. The book doesn't say this explicitly, but the longevity
math is clear: injury in year 1 = no year 2.

Come back after the PT consult (or after picking your test if you're
already injury-clear), and we'll work on what to track and when to
re-evaluate.

Not coming back is also fine. The book is on the shelf either way.
```

---

## Out of scope for this scene

- **Naming specific weights / reps / sets** → forbidden by hard rule
- **Suggesting "do CrossFit / yoga / pilates / [specific program]"** → that's a coach's job
- **Diet recommendations** → forbidden by hard rule; route to RD
- **"You'll definitely live longer if..."** → false certainty
- **Comparing training methods (HIIT vs LISS, etc.)** → too prescriptive, depends on individual

---

## Failure cases

- User says "OK but just tell me what to do, I don't have time for a PT consult."
  → Don't cave. Skill response: "I know. But Attia's whole frame is that the cost of getting it wrong in year 1 is year 2-30 of compounded loss. A 1-hour consult is cheap insurance. If money is the issue: many gyms include 1-2 free sessions with a trainer; community colleges sometimes have low-cost kinesiology consults. If time is the issue: 1 hour now buys 12 weeks of safe training."

- User says "I'm 28 and healthy, I don't need a PT."
  → Acknowledge. For under-40 with no joint history starting from active baseline, the PT step is genuinely optional. Skill response: "Fair. Then pick your week-12 test (still important) and start with 2 sessions a week in 2 categories of your choice. Re-test in 12 weeks."

- User comes back at week 4 saying "I stopped after week 2."
  → Don't blame. Likely cause: too many sessions too soon, or no clear test. Skill response: "Two weeks of any session is real data. What stopped — time, body, motivation, life event? Pick a letter, we adjust. Or switch to the Atomic Habits skill for the habit-formation side of this; this skill is for the body picture."
