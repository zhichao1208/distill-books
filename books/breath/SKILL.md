# Breath · skill

For people who suspect their breathing might be making other things worse — and want the evidence-graded subset of Nestor's tour, not the wellness-Instagram version.

## Cold-start

### A · Slow exhale (the best-evidenced practice)

```
This is the move with the strongest evidence in the book.

The protocol:
  - Inhale through the NOSE for ~4 seconds
  - Exhale through the nose or pursed lips for ~6 seconds
  - That's it. About 6 breaths per minute. ~5 minutes is enough
    for measurable HRV / parasympathetic activation.

Why this works (Laborde et al. 2022, Neurosci Biobehav Rev):
slow paced breathing at resonance frequency (around 0.1 Hz, or
6 breaths/min) synchronizes respiration with the baroreflex,
maximizes HRV amplitude, and lowers blood pressure acutely.
Across multiple meta-analyses the effects on state anxiety
and HRV are consistent.

The longer-exhale-than-inhale piece adds vagal-brake activation
(parasympathetic tone). The exact ratio matters less than just
making the exhale longer.

What this is NOT:
  - A treatment for anxiety disorder, panic disorder, or PTSD
    (those need clinical care; slow breathing is an adjunct)
  - A magic on/off switch — first few times may feel awkward;
    that's fine
  - To be done while driving in the first weeks (rare cases of
    mild dizziness)

Concrete: try 5 minutes now. Note what changes.
```

### B · Sleep-onset stack (nasal + slow exhale)

```
The bedtime stack — both pieces have decent evidence:

1. Nasal breathing as default. If chronically nasally obstructed,
   see an ENT (deviated septum, polyps, allergies are common and
   treatable). Don't try to power-through with willpower if your
   nose doesn't work.

2. Slow exhale before sleep. Same protocol as Cold-start A,
   ~5-10 minutes lying down. The parasympathetic shift is the
   reason it helps sleep onset.

What about mouth-taping?

Honest answer: it's the practice that broke through to TikTok
and the one sleep medicine has pushed back on hardest.

  - No RCTs in healthy adults (Fangmeyer 2024 scoping review:
    only 9 studies, mostly small, mostly in already-diagnosed
    mild OSA on PAP)
  - AASM has NOT endorsed
  - Aspiration risk + undiagnosed-apnea risk
  - One small pilot in mild OSA (Huang & Hsu 2022) does not
    generalize

The right sequence: if you snore (especially loudly), have
witnessed apnea, gasp / choke at night, are sleepy in the day,
have hypertension or AFib — get a sleep study FIRST. Not tape.

If a sleep study is clean and you simply mouth-breathe at night,
mouth-taping is currently weak evidence. Some people try it
under their own risk-tolerance. The skill doesn't recommend it
as default — it routes to clinical evaluation first.

What's your bedtime situation? Snoring, daytime sleepiness, BMI,
allergies, witnessed apnea — give me the picture.
```

### C · Nasal-only workout trial (athletic case)

```
Athletes asking about nasal breathing have a real-but-narrow
evidence base.

What's defensible:
  - Most people can nasal-breathe at low-to-moderate intensity
    (Zone 1-2). At threshold and above, mouth + nose becomes
    necessary. Pushing nasal-only at high intensity is
    counterproductive.
  - Training nasal-only at low intensity may improve CO2
    tolerance and Zone 2 efficiency for some people. Small
    studies, mixed.
  - Nasal NO during inhale (Lundberg et al.) has real physiology
    behind it.

What's overhyped:
  - "Mouth-tape during workouts" — mostly hype.
  - "Nasal-only marathons" — possible for elite-tier endurance
    athletes after long adaptation; not a starting protocol.

The 4-week trial:
  - Week 1: All Zone 1-2 work nasal-only. Slow down if you have
    to.
  - Week 2-4: Keep Zone 1-2 nasal; mouth-breathe in tempo and
    intervals as normal.
  - After 4 weeks, notice: easier nasal at given pace? Lower HR
    at given pace? Both are signal.

What to NOT do:
  - Nasal-only above threshold (counterproductive, hypoxia risk)
  - Nasal-only in heat / humidity if it's not working (your nose
    has limits)
  - Mouth-tape during exercise (no, just no)
  - Wim Hof / tummo cycles before water entry — multiple
    drowning deaths. NEVER.

What's your sport and what's your current breathing pattern?
```

## When this skill is the wrong fit — routing

| Your situation | Where to go |
|---|---|
| Suspected sleep apnea (loud snoring, witnessed apnea, daytime sleepiness, BMI > 30) | Sleep medicine — request polysomnography |
| Asthma exacerbation / COPD | Pulmonologist — never replace inhaler with breathwork |
| Anxiety disorder / panic disorder / PTSD | Mental health clinician; breathing tools are adjunct, not treatment |
| Cardiovascular disease / recent stroke / pregnancy | Cardiologist / OB — many breathwork practices contraindicated |
| Pediatric snoring / mouth-breathing | Pediatric ENT (often adenoids / tonsils) — not adult tactics |
| Active trauma / dissociation | Trauma-informed therapist — slow breathing can trigger for some |

Adjacent skills on this shelf:

- **Why We Sleep** — for sleep architecture, alcohol/caffeine timing, CBT-I routing for chronic insomnia
- **Outlive** — for the broader Medicine 3.0 / Centenarian Decathlon health-frame

Breath tactics layer on top of clinical care. They never replace it.
