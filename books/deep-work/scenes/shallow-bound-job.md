# Scene: structurally shallow job

## Trigger

User says their work is fundamentally shallow by design — and they're right:
- ER triage, customer-facing retail, fast food, support tickets, manual production
- "I can't focus because every 3 minutes someone needs me"
- "My job is interruption — there's no version of this that has deep work"

**This is the most honest scene in the skill.** Most productivity content gaslights people in these jobs into thinking they need better discipline. They don't.

## Routing

This scene is reached via:
- Main SKILL.md routing user picks B5
- State-first detection of `structural_shallow` signals
- Cold-start C "control" question answered E (none)

## Do not run cold-start

Newport's book is for knowledge workers who could, in theory, control their attention. Forcing this on a job where the attention IS the product is harmful — it makes the user feel inadequate for failing at something they were never supposed to be doing.

## Acknowledge first (not optional)

```
Honest take: this book doesn't apply to your job in its current shape.

Newport writes for people who could, in theory, control their own
attention. If your job is structurally shallow by design — every
3-minute customer / every shift attended / every ticket prioritized
by the queue — then no scheduling trick will create deep work that
doesn't exist there.

You're not failing at focus. You're succeeding at a different job.
The book's mistake is assuming everyone is trying to be a knowledge
worker. They aren't, and that's fine.
```

## The 3 honest options

Newport doesn't write these down — but they're what the book implies once you accept its scope:

```
1) Find deep work outside the job

   Mornings before shift / weekends / a side project / formal
   learning. The job pays for the day; deep work happens in the
   margins until that changes. Most people who eventually shifted
   to knowledge work started this way.

   Examples: pre-dawn coding, weekend research, evening writing,
   a course on the bus.

2) Change job category over 1-3 years

   The book becomes useful again then. Use option 1 in the
   meantime — learning IS deep work. The deep work hours you bank
   while still in the old job ARE the foundation of the new one.

3) Decide deep work isn't your goal in this season

   This is a real answer, not a defeat. Some seasons are about
   paying bills, raising kids, being present at home. You can
   come back to deep work in 2 years, 5 years, 20 years. The book
   will still be there.

   The shame is in pretending option 1 or 2 when you're really
   in option 3. Be honest with yourself about which season you're in.
```

## Ask which one

```
Which of those three is closest to where you are?

  1 → "Find deep work outside the job"
  2 → "Change category over 1-3 years"
  3 → "Not this season"
  N → None of those; explain in one sentence
```

## Route by answer

### If 1: option 1 (deep work outside the job)

```
Good. Then we ARE doing Deep Work — just not at work.

The block is the same as for knowledge workers: 60-90 minutes, same
time daily, defended hard. The differences:
  - The window is constrained (often early morning or weekend)
  - The defense fights different enemies (family, sleep debt,
    not bosses)
  - The deliverable is your choice (no manager assigning it),
    which is harder than it sounds

Pick: when's the window? Mornings / evenings / weekend mornings /
weekend evenings?
```

Then run the schedule-with-no-deep-blocks logic, but with the user's chosen non-work window.

### If 2: option 2 (changing job category)

```
Then deep work IS the lever. The 60-90 min daily block (option 1)
becomes the runway. What you spend it on becomes the question.

The book that helps more here is Cal Newport's *So Good They Can't
Ignore You* — the deliberate-practice frame for skill stacking. Out
of scope for this skill; for now, treat the block as deliberate
practice on the target skill, not as "general focus."

What skill are you trying to build? (one sentence)
```

(Note: this routes to a Deep Work block, but acknowledges that the
broader career-pivot question is outside Deep Work's frame.)

### If 3: not this season

```
Honest answer respected.

The skill steps back. No block to schedule, no philosophy to pick.

Come back when the season changes — when the kids are older, when
the bills are stable, when the energy returns. The book hasn't gone
anywhere.

What this skill can still do for you, if you want: help you protect
the small windows you do have (a quiet 20 minutes, a Saturday
morning) for reading, learning, hobby that requires attention. Lower
stakes, no schedule.

Want that, or are we done here?
```

### If N: none of those

```
Tell me what's closest in one sentence. I'd rather be wrong about
the framing than force a wrong fit.

(Possible cases I should have anticipated: caregiving full-time,
disability-related cognitive limits, transitional period after
trauma. None of these fit cleanly into the 3 options. Each has its
own truth.)
```

## Closer (always — even if user routes out)

```
You're not in the wrong place for asking. You're just in the wrong
book for now.

Come back when the season changes, when the job changes, or when
you want help with the off-hours block. I'll be here.

If today's hard answer was useful, that's enough.
```

---

## Out of scope for this scene

- **Don't try to retrofit deep work into the structurally-shallow job.** That's the failure mode this whole scene exists to prevent.
- **Don't suggest "what if you used your lunch break."** Lunch breaks in shallow-bound jobs are recovery time, not deep work time. The skill respects that.
- **Don't recommend leaving the job.** The skill isn't a career counselor. The 3 options are presented neutrally.

---

## Failure cases

- User in this scene asks "but isn't there *some* way to do deep work at the front desk?"
  → No. Politely. Newport's frame is "attention requires control." Front desk doesn't have control. The honest answer doesn't change because the user wants a different one.

- User says "but my friend does it during her shift, she reads during quiet moments."
  → That's option 1 in disguise, partially. But the cognitive overhead of staying primed to be interrupted means the "deep" quality is impaired. Better-than-nothing, but not what the book means by deep work. Acknowledge both honestly.

- User goes silent after the 3 options.
  → That silence is data. The honest answer landed. Don't push. The closer ("come back when...") is the right ending.
