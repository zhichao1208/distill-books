## Critical honesty
- **Survivorship bias**: Epstein's case studies are late-specializers who SUCCEEDED. The much larger pool who sampled-and-failed isn't represented. Skill flags in boot.
- **Tiger Woods archetype is mythologized**: Earl Woods used systematic VARIED training, not narrow drills. The "early-specialization-as-only-path" myth Epstein critiques is partly a strawman because the real Tiger story isn't what the public believes.
- **Kind/wicked distinction is useful but boundary is fuzzy** in practice — many real domains are mixed.
- **Book inadvertently risks justifying dilettantism** (chronic non-completion). Skill flags as not_for.

## Concepts deliberately not used
- Strong "everyone should be a generalist" framing (book's actual claim is narrower)
- Range vs SGTCIY as binary "right answer" (they're opposite ends of same axis; user picks by context)

## Differentiation from So Good They Can't Ignore You
Direct opposites on same axis. Range = sample more, late-specialize, breadth. SGTCIY = pick direction, compound deliberately, depth. Most readers benefit from synthesis (Range for "should I specialize early," SGTCIY for "how do I build skill once committed").
