# Scene: inheritance timing — give while alive

## Trigger

User considering: "I want to help my kids financially while I'm alive" / "I'm sitting on money my kids could use NOW" / "Should I give early instead of leaving inheritance?"

This is Perkins's strongest specific claim. Skill carries it carefully.

## After main SKILL.md cold-start C

### 1. The mathematical case (Perkins's version)

```
The math:

  Average parent dies in their 70s-80s. Their inheriting children
  are 45-60.
  
  A 60-year-old with $X has marginal utility for X that's typically
  LOWER than a 30-year-old with the same X. The 60-year-old is
  established, kids grown, lifestyle set, often saving the inheritance
  themselves.
  
  Same X to a 30-year-old can change their trajectory:
  - Down payment on a house (compounding home equity)
  - Time off to have kids without partner having to work
  - Education for their own kids (compounding educational outcome)
  - Starting a business
  - Caregiving for a relative (avoiding career off-ramp)
  - Buying out an unhappy partnership / career

Perkins's claim: shifting some inheritance from "death transfer"
to "peak-utility-age transfer" creates MORE total family wealth
and life satisfaction over generations.

The claim is true on the math IF other conditions hold (see below).
```

### 2. The conditions that must hold

```
Give-while-alive is right ONLY if all these are true:

  ✓ Your own retirement / safety net is genuinely secured
    (not "I think so" — you've actually checked with an advisor)
  ✓ Your family relationships can handle the conversation about
    money openly (not all can; some giving creates worse problems
    than it solves)
  ✓ Your recipient is in a place where the money lands well
    (not active addiction, not abusive partner who will take it,
    not legal entanglement that exposes the gift)
  ✓ You've considered tax implications (annual gift exclusions,
    lifetime exemptions, jurisdiction-specific rules)
  ✓ You're prepared for the relationship dynamic to shift (giving
    money changes things; this isn't always negative but it's
    real)

If any of these fail, "give while alive" is the wrong move OR
needs heavy structuring (trusts, conditions, professional advice).
The skill is honest: the book underweights how often these
conditions fail.
```

### 3. Practical structure for giving

```
If you've decided to give and conditions hold:

  A. ESTIMATE: roughly how much will you likely leave at death
     under current trajectory? Be realistic, not optimistic.
     
  B. DEDICATE: what fraction could be moved forward? Perkins
     suggests significant fractions (50%+) for over-savers; that's
     aggressive. Most users start at 10-30% to test the dynamic.
     
  C. STRUCTURE: lump sum vs annual? Tied to specific events
     (wedding, first home, baby, education)? Or unconditional?
     - Specific events: easier to time, clearer purpose
     - Unconditional: better signals trust and avoids surveillance
       dynamic; harder if you have control tendencies
     
  D. TALK FIRST: have the conversation with recipient(s) BEFORE
     the transfer. The Perkins book repeatedly says this and
     research on inheritance bears it out. Surprise transfers
     produce more family conflict than telegraphed ones.
     
  E. CHECK PROFESSIONAL: even simple gifts can have unexpected
     tax / legal implications. A short consult with an estate
     attorney or tax advisor pays for itself.
```

### 4. The conversation script (the hardest part)

```
Most parents are bad at the money conversation. Some opening
phrasings that work:

  "I've been thinking about what we leave you. I'd rather you
  have some of it at a time when it changes your life, instead
  of all at 60 when you don't need it as much. Let me tell you
  what I'm thinking and we can talk about whether it fits."

  "If you had $X now, would it change anything important — a
  down payment, time off when the baby's small, a career move
  you can't afford to make? I'd like to give you that if so."

  "Here's what I'm comfortable giving and not affecting our own
  security. Here's what would be useful to you. Let's match those."

What NOT to do:
  - Surprise transfer with no discussion (confusing, sometimes
    creates suspicion)
  - Strings attached without clarity ("we expect you to use this
    for X" — if so, say it clearly; don't hint)
  - Pretend the money has no relational weight (it does; honesty
    helps)
  - Wait until you're certain (you won't be; the conditions never
    perfectly align)
```

### Closer

```
This is the most consequential decision the book is asking you to
make. Two specific actions if you're going forward:

  1. Consult a fee-only financial advisor THIS MONTH to verify
     the safety threshold is intact after the planned giving.
  2. Schedule the conversation with your recipient(s) within 30
     days. Use one of the opening phrases above or your own
     version. Don't transfer until after the conversation.

If you're not ready for both — pause. The book isn't urgent enough
to skip these.

If after consult + conversation you still want to proceed:
  - Make the first transfer specific (amount, purpose if any,
    timing)
  - See how the family dynamic responds over 6 months
  - Calibrate the next gift based on what you learned

Come back if useful. Tell me what the conversation was like. Or
don't — these are family decisions, not skill ones.
```

---

## When the skill should push back

- User wants to give while alive without checking their own runway → push back hard; safety first
- User's recipient is in active addiction or abuse → push back; "well-intentioned harm" is real
- User's giving plan is to control the recipient → push back; this isn't gift giving, it's leverage
- User's family relationships are chronically toxic → flag that money won't fix relational issues; might make them worse
- User wants to skip the conversation → push back; book is clear that surprise transfers create the most conflict

## Out of scope

- Specific tax / estate structuring (lawyer's job)
- Trust structures, family LLCs, gifting strategies (estate planner)
- Cross-border inheritance issues (specialized counsel)
- Divorce-related giving (lawyer first, financial second)
