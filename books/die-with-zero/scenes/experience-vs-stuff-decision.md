# Scene: experience vs stuff decision

## Trigger

User asking: "Should I buy [thing] or spend on [experience]?" / "Am I just being materialistic?" / "Is the trip worth more than the new [object]?"

## After main SKILL.md cold-start (probably B)

### The Memory Dividend framing

```
Perkins's idea: experiences pay a "memory dividend" over time.

You take a trip at 30. For 50 years, you remember it. The trip's
value isn't just the trip — it's the trip + 50 years of remembering.
You buy a luxury object at 30. The object brings pleasure briefly,
then fades into ambient infrastructure ("nice but doesn't move me").
At 80, you don't tell stories about the object; you tell stories
about the trip.

Empirically (loosely supported by happiness research, Norton/Dunn
and others): experiences generally yield more durable wellbeing
per dollar than possessions, BECAUSE:
  - Experiences integrate into identity ("I'm someone who...")
  - Experiences resist hedonic adaptation longer
  - Experiences generate social bonding (shared with others)
  - Experiences strengthen with retelling

BUT:
  - Not all experiences. A forgotten trip pays no dividend.
  - Not all possessions are equal. A musical instrument that you
    play for 30 years yields its own kind of compound dividend.
  - The categories blur (a kitchen renovation = both possession
    and ongoing experience of cooking; a high-end mattress = a
    possession that affects every day's experience of sleep)
```

### The diagnostic for a specific decision

```
You're choosing between X experience and Y purchase. Walk through:

  1. Memory dividend test:
     - At 80, looking back, will the experience generate a story?
     - At 80, looking back, will the purchase generate a feeling
       you remember?
     Usually experience wins. Sometimes the purchase wins
     (childhood camera you carry for life; a tool you've used for
     decades; a piece of art you live with).
  
  2. Window-closing test:
     - Is the experience time-limited? (Travel before kids; physical
       capacity in your 30s/40s; aging parents who won't be there
       in 5 years)
     - Is the purchase available indefinitely? (Most physical objects
       can be bought later; some experiences cannot)
     If experience window is closing AND purchase is available later
     → experience wins.
  
  3. Integration test:
     - Does the experience FIT your life right now (you have time,
       people to share it with, capacity to engage)?
     - Does the purchase ADD to your life or just decorate it?
     A trip your partner doesn't want or you're too exhausted to
     enjoy ≠ memory dividend.
  
  4. Necessity test:
     - Is there a genuine NEED behind either? (replacement laptop
       for work; medical equipment; necessary infrastructure)
     If yes, that's not the decision — that's just paying for what
     you need. The framing only applies to discretionary spending.
```

### When the purchase actually wins

Skill is honest that "always pick experience" is wrong:

```
Cases where the purchase is the right answer:

  - The object is used daily for 5+ years (good mattress, ergonomic
    chair, the right cooking knife, decent running shoes for a
    runner, an instrument you play, a tool you use weekly)
  - The object enables OTHER experiences (a camera enables travel
    photography you love; a bike enables daily rides)
  - The object replaces a worse one that's actively reducing your
    quality of life (broken chair causing back pain; outdated 
    computer crashing daily)
  - The object has heritage / sentimental dimension you'll integrate
    into life (a piece of family-style art, a quality piece of
    furniture you'll use 30 years)

The book's "experiences > stuff" is correctly applied to:
  - Discretionary single purchases
  - Status / display items
  - Things you'll use briefly then store
  - Things you're buying because it's what people in your reference
    group buy

It's NOT correctly applied to functional infrastructure or to
objects you'll integrate deeply over time.
```

### Closer

```
For your specific decision:

  Run the 4-test diagnostic above. Most ties break toward the
  experience UNLESS the purchase passes the "daily-use for 5+
  years" or "enables other experiences" tests.

If both pass: do whichever is genuinely more memorable at 80.
Often it's a question only you can answer.

Concrete move this week: write down both options + the 4-test
result. Sleep on it. Decide. Don't deliberate forever — that's
its own cost.

Come back if useful.
```

---

## Out of scope

- Investment-grade purchases (art, watches, wine as financial assets) — different frame; not "stuff" in Perkins's sense
- Necessary purchases (medical, housing, transportation if no alternative) — not discretionary
- Gift purchases for others (different dynamics, see inheritance-timing scene)
- Purchases that hide an underlying issue (compulsive shopping; that's therapy territory)

---

## Failure modes

- **User uses framing to justify already-decided expensive purchase.** Skill should notice: "It sounds like you've already decided. That's fine. I'm not the spending police. If the decision is made, the framing is post-hoc rationalization — which is normal but doesn't need this skill."

- **User uses framing to delay all spending indefinitely.** Skill should notice: "Endless deliberation about 'is it memory-dividend enough' is its own form of over-saving — by deliberation rather than by hoarding. The decision itself takes some weight; don't drown it in framing."

- **User reveals significant under-saving while asking about discretionary purchase.** Skill routes back to PoM / financial planning — discretionary spending question is the wrong question when basics aren't covered.
