# Scene: over-saver who can't spend

## Trigger

User reports: "I have money but I feel guilty spending it" / "I keep saving even though I have enough" / "I'm 50 and afraid to enjoy what I worked for."

Most common scenario for users this skill is the right tool for.

## After main SKILL.md cold-start A

### 1. Verify the user is actually over-saving (not under-saving with denial)

Skill must NOT skip this check:

```
Before we go further: are you actually over-saving, or telling
yourself you are while still being under-saved?

Honest gut answers:
  - If I lost income tomorrow, could I cover 6 months expenses
    from cash/liquid savings? (yes / probably / no)
  - Am I on track for retirement at my current spending?
    (clear yes / probably / unclear / no)
  - Do I have any high-interest debt? (no / yes)
  - Is my income reasonably stable in the next 5 years?
    (yes / probably / no)

If all four are clear yes — proceed.
If any are "probably" — verify with a fee-only financial advisor
  BEFORE applying any "spend more" framing.
If any are "no" — go to Psychology of Money skill. Die With Zero
  is the wrong tool.
```

### 2. Name the specific over-saving behavior

```
"I have money but can't spend" is usually one of:

  A. Defensive over-saving (fear-driven): "What if I lose my job /
     get sick / have an emergency"
  B. Identity over-saving (frugal-as-self): "I am a person who
     doesn't spend frivolously"
  C. Comparison over-saving: "Others spend less than this"
  D. Childhood-rooted over-saving: parents were under-savers, you
     overcorrected
  E. Couple dynamic: one partner is the over-saver, the other isn't
  F. Genuine uncertainty: you don't actually know if you're over-
     saving, you're just defaulting

Pick a letter — that determines what the actual work is.
```

### 3. Treat each type differently

**A (fear-driven)**:
```
The fear was useful in your 20s-30s. It often outlives its
usefulness — doesn't update when the runway changes.

Concrete move: write down your actual safety threshold (6 months
expenses + retirement runway). Compare to current. The gap is
deployable. The fear of spending past that line is the irrational
part, not the prudence below it.

The fear won't go away with one decision. It eases with reps —
spending small amounts deliberately and noticing nothing bad
happens. Then larger. Over months, the recalibration happens.

This week: pick one experience under €1000 you've been postponing.
Book it. Notice your fear response. Notice you survive.
```

**B (identity-as-frugal)**:
```
This is harder than (A) because the over-saving is wound into your
self-concept. The decision to spend conflicts with who you think
you are.

The Perkins move: separate "frugal as default" (good) from "frugal
as identity that prevents living" (bad). Frugality is a value, not
a personality trait. You can be a frugal person who deliberately
spends on high-memory-dividend experiences. The frugality serves
the spending, not the other way around.

This week: identify ONE category where your frugality is hurting
you (the experience never taken, the gift never given). Override
in that one category. Stay frugal everywhere else.
```

**C (comparison)**:
```
You're benchmarking against others, but their financial picture is
opaque. Their lifestyle could be:
  - Inherited / supported (looks like spending, isn't)
  - Debt-financed (looks like spending, isn't sustainable)
  - Actually appropriate for THEIR income / situation
  - Genuinely less spent because they value different things

The Perkins move: stop benchmarking against others entirely. Bench-
mark against YOUR life's experiences and YOUR Net Worth Curve.

This week: ask yourself "what would I do this year if I weren't
checking what others do?" Notice the gap between that answer and
your actual plans.
```

**D (childhood-rooted)**:
```
Common pattern: parents were under-savers (or had a scarcity event
that shaped your childhood). You overcorrected. The over-correction
served you well for decades; now it's harming you.

This is partly a therapy question, not just a money question. The
financial work doesn't fully release until the underlying childhood
material is acknowledged.

This week: notice when the over-saving impulse fires. What's the
underlying feeling — fear of being like your parents? Shame about
spending? That's the work. Skill can't do the therapy; it can flag
the pattern.

Modest concrete move: one spending decision a month done deliberately
WITH the discomfort, not waiting for the discomfort to pass.
```

**E (couple)**:
```
Skill doesn't fix relationships. But it can frame the conversation:

If one partner is over-saving and the other isn't, the conversation
is not "who's right." It's:
  - What's our shared safety threshold? (specific number)
  - Above that threshold, what does each of us value spending on?
  - Are there experiences we both want that aren't happening?
  - Are there asymmetries (one of us values experiences more,
    the other security more) — and how do we share both?

The Perkins frame works for couples only if both partners are at
least directionally aligned. If you're not, this is therapy
territory, not skill territory.

This week: have ONE specific conversation with your partner about
ONE specific experience neither of you has booked despite both
wanting it. Not "we should spend more"; "should we book [trip] for
spring?"
```

**F (genuine uncertainty)**:
```
The honest answer: you don't actually know.

You're operating from default ("save more is safer") without
knowing your actual position. This is fixable.

Concrete move: this month, do an actual financial assessment.
Options:
  - Fee-only financial advisor (Honorarberater in Germany, NAPFA
    in US, fee-only ones in UK/Australia/etc.) — usually ~$500-1500
    for a planning session
  - A solid spreadsheet on your own (assets, liabilities, projected
    expenses through 90, projected income through 65)
  - One of the better online calculators (FIRECalc, ficalc.app,
    cFIREsim)

Without an actual baseline, "should I spend more" is unanswerable.
The skill recommends getting the baseline before continuing.
```

### Closer

```
You picked a type. The skill's response was specific.

This week, do one thing:
  - A (fear) → book one experience under €1000 you've postponed
  - B (identity) → spend in ONE category where the over-saving costs you
  - C (comparison) → stop benchmarking against others for 30 days
  - D (childhood) → notice the impulse + flag for therapy
  - E (couple) → one specific conversation, one specific item
  - F (uncertainty) → start the actual financial assessment

Come back in a month or whenever. Say "did it / didn't / discovered
something else" — letter is enough.

Not coming back is fine.
```
