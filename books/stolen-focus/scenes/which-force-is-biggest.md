# Scene: which force is biggest

## Trigger

User wants to diagnose which of the ~12 attention-stealing forces affects them most. Specific request, not abstract reframe.

## The 12 forces (organized by category)

Skill walks through these in groups so the user can narrow:

```
Hari names roughly 12 forces. Most people have 2-4 dominant ones.
Let's narrow by category.

GROUP 1 — DIGITAL ENVIRONMENT
  A. Notifications / constant interruption
  B. Engagement-engineered platforms (social, short video, news)
  C. Email / chat as default work pace

GROUP 2 — PHYSICAL CONDITION
  D. Sleep debt (chronic <7h or fragmented)
  E. Diet (sugar / ultra-processed / glucose volatility)
  F. Chronic stress / nervous system dysregulation
  G. Air pollution / environmental toxicants

GROUP 3 — STRUCTURAL / CULTURAL
  H. Open offices / always-on culture
  I. Loss of long-form reading habit
  J. Loss of unstructured free time
  K. Information overload / news anxiety cycle

GROUP 4 — DEVELOPMENTAL / CLINICAL ADJACENT
  L. Childhood factors (for adults reflecting on origin)
  M. ADHD-adjacent symptoms (sometimes clinical, sometimes environmental,
     skill routes to clinician if clinical signs)

Which group hits hardest? A letter is enough.
```

## After group selection, narrow further

Each group has specific diagnostic questions:

### Group 1 (digital)

```
Pick the strongest one:
  A. Notifications — your phone fires 50+ alerts/day; you can't get
     90 min without checking
  B. Engagement engineering — you open Instagram/TikTok/YouTube
     intending 5 min, look up 2 hours later, don't know how
  C. Email/chat as default pace — Slack is the canvas your work
     life paints on, even though you "know" deep work matters

Most common in 2026: B + A combined.

Fix categories:
  - A → notification audit (turn off all except real emergencies);
    Tiny Habits scenes for environment design
  - B → delete apps not block them (you'll re-install but the
    friction pays); Deep Work + Tiny Habits both useful
  - C → harder, structural; depends on your job. Deep Work skill's
    structural scene helps
```

### Group 2 (physical)

```
Pick the strongest:
  D. Sleep debt — under 7h most nights, fragmented, or just feeling
     tired in a "this is normal" way (it's not)
  E. Diet — heavy ultra-processed, sugar crashes, you notice
     attention dipping after meals
  F. Chronic stress — body doesn't relax even on weekends
  G. Air pollution — you live in/near high-pollution area; some
     evidence this affects attention chronically

Fix routes:
  - D → Why We Sleep skill
  - E → Outlive skill for the framing; specialist (RD) for specific
    diet changes
  - F → therapy / lifestyle / sometimes medication; outside this
    skill
  - G → environmental change (air filter at home, where you live,
    masks in heavy traffic); systemic policy
```

### Group 3 (structural)

```
Pick the strongest:
  H. Open office / always-on workplace
  I. Lost the long-form reading muscle (can't read a book chapter
     without checking phone)
  J. No unstructured time (every minute scheduled, no boredom)
  K. News anxiety cycle

Fixes:
  - H → mostly structural; workplace change or remote setup; some
    individual defenses
  - I → re-train the reading muscle: 10 min/day paper books, phone
    in other room, build back the attention span
  - J → calendar 1-2 unstructured hours weekly; explicitly NOT
    productive time; learn to be bored again
  - K → news consumption time-boxed (15 min/day max); curated
    sources not algorithmic; no news in first or last hour of day
```

### Group 4 (clinical-adjacent)

```
Pick:
  L. Looking back at childhood factors — heavy screens, anxiety,
     low free play
  M. Adult ADHD suspicion

  - L → mostly retrospective; the framing matters for processing
    your own history but doesn't change next steps. Therapy is
    appropriate if it's distressing.
  - M → CLINICAL EVALUATION. Hari's environmental frame is helpful
    context but doesn't substitute for evaluation. See psychiatrist
    or psychologist who treats adult ADHD.
```

## Closer

```
You've narrowed to a force. Route:
  - Digital force → Tiny Habits (environment) + Deep Work (operating)
  - Sleep / metabolic → Why We Sleep / Outlive
  - Stress → therapy / lifestyle, outside this skill
  - Structural → workplace conversation or change; Deep Work's
    structural scene
  - Clinical-adjacent → evaluation, this skill flags but doesn't
    treat

The reframe (Stolen Focus's job): you've stopped blaming yourself for
the wrong cause. The operating work (Deep Work / Tiny Habits) is
the next move.

Come back if you want to reassess after some weeks of work in the
named direction. Not coming back is fine.
```

---

## Failure modes

- **User picks "all of the above" and feels overwhelmed.** Reasonable answer; common. Skill says: "Pick ONE for the next 4-6 weeks. Work on it. The compounding from one fixed force often unlocks energy to address the next. Treating all 4 at once = treating none."

- **User wants the skill to fix the force itself.** Skill stays in scope: "I'm the diagnostic skill. Treating the force is Deep Work / Tiny Habits / a clinician. Hand-off is the right move."

- **User reveals the dominant force is one this skill can't address (severe chronic disease, abusive workplace, structural poverty).** Acknowledge honestly: "Some attention-eroding forces aren't 'fix-with-skill' problems. They're life-stage / circumstance / structural problems that need different solutions (medical care, leaving a situation, advocacy). The skill's reframe still matters — you're not undisciplined for having reduced attention in those circumstances — but the fix isn't in this skill or any productivity skill."
