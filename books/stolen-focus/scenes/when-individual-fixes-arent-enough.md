# Scene: when individual fixes aren't enough

## Trigger

User has done some individual work (deleted apps, set boundaries, even Deep Work / Tiny Habits) and asks: "What about the bigger picture?" / "I'm one person against an industry, what's the systemic angle?" / "Is changing my behavior even the right level to operate at?"

This is Hari's biggest argument and the skill carries it honestly.

## The honest framing

```
Hari's argument that pushed past Deep Work / Newport:

You cannot out-discipline an industry designed to defeat you.

That's literal. Engagement-optimizing platforms employ thousands of
engineers, run A/B tests on you continuously, evolve their attention-
capture mechanisms faster than your willpower can update. Individual
tactics can recover some of your attention budget — they can't
restore the pre-platform baseline.

The corollary: some changes only happen at the collective level.

Which doesn't mean individual tactics are pointless. It means they're
incomplete.
```

## Three levels of action (be honest about what each can and can't do)

```
LEVEL 1 — INDIVIDUAL
  Tactics: delete apps, move phone, schedule defenses, environment
  design, Deep Work protocols, Tiny Habits.
  
  What it CAN do: recover meaningful attention budget (often 1-3 
  productive hours/day previously bled to distraction). Reduce
  guilt overhead. Build a different baseline.
  
  What it CAN'T do: change the platforms that engineer against you.
  Change the workplace that defaults to always-on. Change the cultural
  baseline of attention erosion. Make the next decade of engagement
  engineering less aggressive.

LEVEL 2 — RELATIONAL / WORKPLACE
  Actions: choose workplaces that have async / right-to-disconnect /
  protected focus norms. Have explicit conversations with partners /
  family about phone-free time. Be the person in your team who
  models attention.
  
  What it CAN do: shape the local micro-environment around you.
  Build pockets of saner attention culture.
  
  What it CAN'T do: change the industry. Change the default.

LEVEL 3 — POLITICAL / COLLECTIVE
  Actions: vote for / advocate for privacy regulation, algorithm
  regulation, attention-economy regulation. EU has the DSA and DMA;
  US is mostly behind. Support institutions doing attention research
  (Center for Humane Technology, certain academic centers). Support
  schools that prioritize free play and reduce screen exposure.
  
  What it CAN do (over decades): change the default. Build a 
  cultural baseline where attention is treated as a resource worth
  protecting collectively.
  
  What it CAN'T do (in your daily life this week): help you focus
  tomorrow.
```

## What this means for what to actually do

```
The honest sequence:

  1. Do the individual work NOW. It's where you have most agency
     and the fastest feedback. Don't wait for society to fix this.

  2. Do the relational / workplace work over months. Pick fights
     you can win; model attention without lecturing; choose
     environments deliberately when you have the choice.

  3. Participate in the political conversation over years. Vote
     for candidates and platforms that take the attention economy
     seriously. Support policy organizations. Read accurately about
     what's happening in EU regulatory bodies (DSA, DMA, AI Act).

  4. ACCEPT that some baseline attention erosion is collective and
     won't fix in your lifetime. You're operating in a less 
     attentionally-rich environment than humans had 30 years ago.
     This isn't a moral failing; it's a fact. Adjust expectations.

This skill doesn't have a tactic for level 3. It just refuses to
pretend levels 1 + 2 are sufficient.
```

## Some practical level-2 moves

```
Specific local-environment moves that aren't quite individual but
aren't policy:

  - Have one explicit conversation with your manager: "I'd like 
    one 90-min block daily where I'm not on Slack. Here's what I'll
    deliver in exchange."
  - Family agreement on phone-free meals (one meal/day if all-meals
    is unrealistic)
  - For parents: not modeling distraction in front of kids (kids
    learn attention from watching adults, more than from being lectured)
  - Pick a workplace deliberately for its attention culture — async-
    default companies exist; remote-first companies often do; ask
    in interviews about meeting load and focus norms
  - For couples: agree on "phone in other room" rules for specific
    hours, mutually applied

These are not big policy changes. They're micro-environment changes
that shift the people around you, which over time shifts the
culture around you.
```

## Honest about the limits

```
A confession the skill makes:

We are losing attention as a culture. Not because individuals are
weak, but because the value-extraction technologies have outpaced 
the value-preservation technologies. The optimists hope this 
oscillates back over decades; the pessimists think it continues to
worsen.

In either case: your individual work matters for your individual 
life. Your collective participation matters for the next 
generation's baseline. Neither replaces the other.

If you want concrete daily tactics, go to Deep Work skill or Tiny
Habits skill. If you want the diagnostic reframe (so you stop
blaming yourself), this skill's first scene did it. If you want
to think about systemic change, the books to read are different
(Cal Newport's *Slow Productivity*, Tristan Harris's work, 
Tim Wu's *The Attention Merchants*, Shoshana Zuboff's *Age of 
Surveillance Capitalism*).

Different books for different levels. This skill is one piece.
```

     (one organization to follow, one regulation to track, one

Come back to the diagnostic / individual work whenever useful.
